## Question 1 - Levels of Processing [recall]

> Rank these encoding tasks from shallowest to deepest: (a) "Does this word rhyme with cat?" (b) "Is this word in capital letters?" (c) "Would this word fit in a sentence about your weekend?"

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> Shallowest to deepest: **(b) → (a) → (c)**
> - (b) Structural (visual form) = **shallowest**
> - (a) Phonological (sound) = **intermediate**
> - (c) Semantic + self-referential = **deepest** → best long-term retention

---

## Question 2 - Serial Position [recall]

> A professor reads a list of 12 vocabulary words. Students are tested immediately. Which words are most likely recalled, and why?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> - **First words** (primacy effect): Well-recalled because they were rehearsed and transferred to **long-term memory**
> - **Last words** (recency effect): Well-recalled because they are still active in **working memory**
> - **Middle words**: Worst recall — not rehearsed enough for LTM, and displaced from WM

---

## Question 3 - Serial Position (Delay) [application]

> The same professor now delays the test by 20 minutes and has students count backward from 100 first. Predict how recall will change.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> - **Primacy effect**: **Remains** — those words are in LTM, unaffected by the delay
> - **Recency effect**: **Disappears** — the delay + counting task clears working memory, so those last words are no longer active
> - Result: Only a primacy effect, no recency effect

## Question 4 - LTM Types [recall]

> Classify each: (a) knowing how to ride a bike, (b) remembering your first day of high school, (c) knowing that Paris is in France, (d) feeling faster to recognize "butter" after seeing "bread."

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> - (a) Bike riding → **Procedural memory** (implicit)
> - (b) First day of high school → **Episodic memory** (explicit)
> - (c) Paris is in France → **Semantic memory** (explicit)
> - (d) "Butter" recognized faster after "bread" → **Priming** (implicit)

## Question 5 - Patient HM [recall]

> Describe Patient HM's memory deficits and what his case proved about memory storage.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> HM had bilateral hippocampal removal. Result: severe **anterograde amnesia** (cannot form new long-term declarative memories). He could do a working memory task nearly normally but would have **no memory of doing it** after a break. Crucially, his **procedural/implicit memory was intact** — he could learn new motor skills but had no recollection of practicing them. This proved that **different memory systems are neurologically distinct**: hippocampus is critical for explicit/declarative memory but NOT required for implicit/procedural learning.

## Question 6 - Encoding Specificity [application]

> A student always studies with coffee and music. On exam day, the room is silent and they have no coffee. Why might they perform worse, and what principle explains this?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> The **encoding specificity principle** (Tulving): memory is best when **retrieval cues match encoding cues**. The student encoded material with coffee + music as part of the context. Without those cues at retrieval, some memory associations are less accessible. This is an example of **context-dependent memory** — the external environment at encoding serves as a retrieval cue.

## Question 7 - Emotional Memory [recall]

> What role does the amygdala play in memory, and why might this explain "flashbulb memories"?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> The **amygdala** boosts memory encoding and consolidation during **emotionally arousing or stressful** situations. This explains flashbulb memories: a highly emotional event (e.g., 9/11, a personal tragedy) triggers amygdala activation → stronger, more vivid encoding → the memory feels permanently burned in. However, despite *feeling* highly accurate, flashbulb memories are still subject to forgetting and distortion over time.

---

## Question 8 - State-Dependent Memory [application]

> A researcher notices that people who learn a word list while mildly anxious recall it better when anxious again at test, compared to when calm. What memory principle does this illustrate?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **State-dependent memory**: the internal physiological/emotional state at encoding serves as a retrieval cue. When the retrieval state matches the encoding state (anxious + anxious), memory is better than when states mismatch (anxious encoding + calm retrieval). This is a form of the encoding specificity principle applied to internal states.

## Question 9 - Memory as Reconstruction [recall]

> Why do psychologists say memory is "reconstructive" rather than like a "video recorder"?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> Memory is not a faithful playback of events — it is an **active reconstruction**. When we remember, we reassemble fragments using:
> - What we actually encoded (incomplete)
> - General knowledge and schemas
> - Post-event information
> - Current beliefs and expectations
> This is why memories can be inaccurate, distorted, or entirely false. A video recorder captures everything accurately; human memory is more like an artist's interpretation — shaped by context, meaning, and current knowledge.

## Question 10 - Analysis [analysis]

> Compare the memory of Patient HM and a person with severe retrograde amnesia (but intact anterograde memory) on: ability to form new explicit memories, recall of old explicit memories, and implicit/procedural learning.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> | Ability | Patient HM (anterograde amnesia) | Retrograde Amnesia |
> |---------|-----|-----|
> | Form new explicit memories | **Severely impaired** | Intact |
> | Recall old explicit memories | Mostly intact (pre-surgery) | **Severely impaired** |
> | Implicit/procedural learning | **Intact** | Intact |
>
> This double dissociation demonstrates that encoding new memories and retrieving old memories rely on at least partially separate systems, and both are distinct from implicit/procedural memory which appears more robust to hippocampal damage.
