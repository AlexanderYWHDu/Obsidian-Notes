## Question 1 - Term Identification [recall]

> In Pavlov's experiment, label each element: food, bell (after conditioning), salivation to food, salivation to bell.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> - Food → **US** (Unconditioned Stimulus — naturally triggers salivation)
> - Salivation to food → **UR** (Unconditioned Response)
> - Bell (after conditioning) → **CS** (Conditioned Stimulus — former NS)
> - Salivation to bell → **CR** (Conditioned Response)

## Question 2 - New Scenario Mapping [application]

> A child is bitten by a dog (causing fear). Now, the child becomes afraid whenever they see any dog. Map: bite, fear from bite, any dog, fear from seeing a dog.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> - Bite → **US**
> - Fear from bite → **UR**
> - Dog → **NS** before conditioning → **CS** after conditioning
> - Fear from seeing dog → **CR**

## Question 3 - Extinction & Spontaneous Recovery [recall]

> A dog has been classically conditioned to salivate to a bell. The bell is then rung many times without food. The salivation disappears. Two weeks later, the bell rings and the dog salivates again briefly. Name these two events and explain what the second event implies.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> - Bell without food → CR disappears = **Extinction**
> - CR returns after rest = **Spontaneous Recovery**
> - Implication: Extinction does NOT erase the original CS–US association. The learned connection still exists in memory; extinction only suppresses the response temporarily. This is why conditioned fears can return after appearing to be overcome.

---

## Question 4 - Generalization & Discrimination [application]

> Little Albert was conditioned to fear white rats. He also shows fear toward white rabbits and fur coats. Later, he shows no fear toward a wooden block. Name the two phenomena.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> - Fear toward white rabbits and fur coats → **Generalization** (similar stimuli to CS elicit CR)
> - No fear toward wooden block → **Discrimination** (distinguishes CS from irrelevant stimuli that were never paired with US)

## Question 5 - Acquisition Conditions [recall]

> What three conditions make acquisition most effective in classical conditioning?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> 1. The **NS precedes the US** (CS comes first)
> 2. The NS is **close in time** to the US (short delay between them)
> 3. There are **many pairings** (multiple trials)

---

## Question 6 - Taste Aversion [application]

> Maria eats sushi for the first time, then develops severe food poisoning 4 hours later. She never ate sushi before. Now she can't stand the smell of sushi. Identify what violated normal acquisition rules.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> This is a **Conditioned Taste Aversion (CTA)** / Garcia Effect.
> Violations of normal acquisition rules:
> - **Only one pairing** (not multiple trials)
> - **4-hour delay** between CS (sushi taste) and US (nausea) — normally CS must be close in time to US
> - **Only taste** caused the aversion, not the restaurant atmosphere or music she was hearing
> This works due to **biological preparedness** — mammals are primed to link taste with illness.

---

## Question 7 - Higher-Order Conditioning [recall]

> Explain higher-order conditioning and give an example.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **Higher-order conditioning**: A CS from one conditioning sequence is used as the "US" to condition a new NS. Example: Bell (CS₁) was paired with food (US) → bell triggers salivation. Now pair a light (NS) with the bell (CS₁) many times → eventually the light alone triggers salivation. The light is now a CS₂.

---

## Question 8 - Biological Preparedness [recall]

> Why is it easier for mammals to associate taste with nausea, but harder to associate light with nausea?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> Due to **biological preparedness** — evolution has shaped the learning systems of each species to efficiently learn associations relevant to their survival. For mammals, **taste is a reliable signal of food safety**; if you eat something and get sick, taste is the most relevant signal to avoid. Birds, which rely more on vision for food selection, more easily associate visual cues with illness. This is hardwired, not learned.

---

## Question 9 - Chemotherapy Application [application]

> Explain how the "diet scapegoat" strategy helps children undergoing chemotherapy avoid developing taste aversions to their regular foods.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> Children undergoing chemotherapy often develop **conditioned taste aversions** to whatever food they ate before their treatment (the food becomes the CS for nausea). The "diet scapegoat" strategy involves giving the child a **novel, unusual food** right before chemo. The child's aversion latches onto the unfamiliar scapegoat food (which they don't eat normally), protecting their regular foods from becoming CSs. This exploits the biological tendency to associate novel tastes with illness.

## Question 10 - Analysis [analysis]

> A therapist is treating a client with a severe dog phobia. The therapist has the client imagine being near dogs in safe settings repeatedly without any attack occurring. Using classical conditioning terminology, explain the mechanism of this treatment and predict a potential complication.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> The treatment uses **extinction**: presenting the CS (dogs) without the US (attack/pain) → the CR (fear) should gradually diminish. However, the complication is **spontaneous recovery**: even after successful extinction, the fear CR may reappear after a period of time, especially if a stressful triggering situation occurs. The original CS-US association is not erased, only suppressed. This is why phobia treatment often requires multiple extinction sessions and may need "booster" exposure sessions over time.
