## Consciousness & Attention

> [!danger]- Trap: Blindsight ≠ Unilateral Neglect
> - **Blindsight**: damage to *primary visual cortex*; patient cannot consciously see but their motor behavior responds to stimuli ("perception without consciousness")
> - **Unilateral Neglect**: damage to *right parietal lobe*; patient is conscious but cannot attend to the *left side of space* — this is an attention problem, NOT a vision problem
> - [[Consciousness & Disorders]]

> [!danger]- Trap: Vegetative vs. Minimally Conscious State
> - **Vegetative**: No awareness; brain imaging can sometimes detect hidden awareness (tennis/home navigation study)
> - **Minimally Conscious**: *Some* awareness, fluctuates between normal consciousness and deep coma
> - We are *not always accurate* at distinguishing them clinically
> - [[Consciousness & Disorders]]

> [!danger]- Trap: Exogenous vs. Endogenous Attention
> - **Exogenous** = *external* captures you (phone notification, jump scare)
> - **Endogenous** = *internal* control (you choose to focus)
> - [[Attention & Dual Processing]]

> [!danger]- Trap: We think we multitask but we don't
> - Heavy multitaskers are actually *worse* at multitasking than light multitaskers
> - Unless one task is highly automatized, we are **task-switching**, not truly parallel
> - Supertaskers exist but are only ~2.5% of population
> - [[Attention & Dual Processing]]

## Sleep

> [!danger]- Trap: Which sleep stage does what
> - **Slow-wave sleep (Non-REM 3)** = critical for **memory consolidation** (especially declarative) → more in **early** part of night
> - **REM sleep** = critical for **cognitive function and dreaming** → more in **later** part of night
> - [[Sleep Stages & Circadian Rhythms]]

> [!danger]- Trap: Circadian disadvantage direction
> - Going **west** is usually MORE detrimental to sports performance (not east — that's jet lag for regular travel)
> - [[Sleep Stages & Circadian Rhythms]]

> [!danger]- Trap: Sleepwalking
> - NOT associated with dreaming (REM sleep behavior disorder IS about dream-enacting)
> - NOT dangerous to wake a sleepwalker
> - No memory of the event
> - [[Sleep Disorders & Dreaming]]

## Classical Conditioning

> [!danger]- Trap: CS vs. NS — when does the NS become a CS?
> - Before conditioning: it's a **NS** (Neutral Stimulus)
> - After repeated pairing with US: it becomes a **CS** (Conditioned Stimulus)
> - CR ≠ UR: the CR is usually *weaker* than the UR
> - [[Classical Conditioning Basics]]

> [!danger]- Trap: Extinction ≠ forgetting
> - Extinction = CS presented without US → CR diminishes, but the original association is **NOT destroyed**
> - Proven by **Spontaneous Recovery**: CR reappears after a rest pause even without retraining
> - [[Pavlov's Processes - Acquisition to Discrimination]]

> [!danger]- Trap: Conditioned Taste Aversion violates normal acquisition rules
> - Works with **only ONE pairing** (not many trials)
> - Works even with **hours of delay** between CS and US
> - Only works with **taste**, not sight/sound + nausea (biological preparedness)
> - [[Biological Constraints & Applications]]

## Operant Conditioning

> [!danger]- Trap: Positive/Negative ≠ Good/Bad
> - **Positive** = *add* a stimulus; **Negative** = *remove* a stimulus
> - **Negative Reinforcement** still INCREASES behavior (it provides relief)
> - Example: car seatbelt beep → put on belt to stop beep = Negative Reinforcement (remove annoying sound → more belt use)
> - [[Operant Conditioning & Reinforcement Matrix]]

> [!danger]- Trap: Classical vs. Operant — which is voluntary?
> - **Classical**: *involuntary*, reflexive; association between two stimuli
> - **Operant**: *voluntary* behavior; association between behavior and its consequence
> - [[Observational Learning]]

> [!danger]- Trap: Punishment caveats
> - Punished behavior is **suppressed, not forgotten** — it only disappears in certain situations
> - Punishment teaches what NOT to do; reinforcement teaches what TO do
> - Intermittent reinforcement = harder to extinguish than continuous
> - [[Schedules Punishment Caveats & Shaping]]

## Memory

> [!danger]- Trap: Primacy vs. Recency Effect — which memory store?
> - **Primacy Effect** → items encoded into **Long-Term Memory** (had rehearsal time)
> - **Recency Effect** → items still in **Working Memory** (most recent)
> - Delayed test or counting task before recall: only **primacy** effect remains (recency disappears because WM was cleared)
> - [[Levels of Processing & Serial Position]]

> [!danger]- Trap: Proactive vs. Retroactive Interference direction
> - **Proactive** (forward-acting): *old* memory interferes with *new* memory (writing last year's date in January)
> - **Retroactive** (backward-acting): *new* memory interferes with *old* memory (filling in old address on a new form)
> - Pro→ old interferes NEW; Retro→ new interferes OLD
> - [[Seven Sins of Memory]]

> [!danger]- Trap: 7 Sins — omission vs. commission
> - **Omission (forgetting)**: Transience, Absentmindedness, Blocking
> - **Commission (distortion)**: Misattribution, Suggestibility, Bias, Persistence
> - All 7 fall out of *normally adaptive* memory functioning
> - [[Seven Sins of Memory]]

> [!danger]- Trap: Anterograde vs. Retrograde Amnesia (Patient HM)
> - **Anterograde**: cannot form *new* long-term memories (HM's main deficit)
> - **Retrograde**: cannot retrieve *old* memories from before the injury
> - HM's implicit/procedural memory was relatively **intact** — he could learn new motor skills but couldn't remember doing them
> - [[Long-Term Memory Types]]
