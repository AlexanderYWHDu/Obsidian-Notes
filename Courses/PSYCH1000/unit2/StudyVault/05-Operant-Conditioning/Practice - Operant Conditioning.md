## Question 1 - Matrix Identification [recall]

> Label each: (a) seatbelt beep stops when buckled → more buckling; (b) speeding ticket → less speeding; (c) taking away tablet when child misbehaves → less misbehavior; (d) getting a gold star for homework → more homework.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> - (a) Remove unpleasant (beep) → increases buckling = **Negative Reinforcement**
> - (b) Add unpleasant (ticket) → decreases speeding = **Positive Punishment**
> - (c) Remove pleasant (tablet) → decreases misbehavior = **Negative Punishment**
> - (d) Add pleasant (star) → increases homework = **Positive Reinforcement**

## Question 2 - Negative Reinforcement Misconception [recall]

> A student says "negative reinforcement is the same as punishment." Correct this misconception.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> This is **incorrect**. Negative reinforcement **INCREASES** behavior (it's still reinforcement). The "negative" refers to *removing* a stimulus — specifically, removing something unpleasant. The behavior that leads to removal of the unpleasant stimulus becomes more likely. Example: taking pain medication (behavior) removes pain (negative stimulus removed) → more medication-taking. This is the opposite of punishment, which decreases behavior.

## Question 3 - OCD Application [application]

> A person with OCD feels intense anxiety (an obsession), then performs a ritual (compulsion) which temporarily relieves the anxiety. Explain why compulsions are so hard to stop using operant conditioning.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> The compulsive behavior is maintained by **negative reinforcement**: the ritual removes the unpleasant obsessive anxiety → the relief reinforces the ritual behavior → compulsion increases. Because relief is immediate and powerful, the negative reinforcement is very strong. Additionally, if the person tries to stop, the anxiety returns (withdrawal of relief), making stopping aversive — this makes the compulsion even more entrenched.

## Question 4 - Schedules of Reinforcement [recall]

> Compare continuous and intermittent reinforcement on: speed of initial learning and resistance to extinction.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> | | Continuous | Intermittent |
> |---|---|---|
> | Speed of initial learning | **Faster** | Slower |
> | Resistance to extinction | **Weaker** (extinguishes quickly) | **Stronger** (very hard to extinguish) |
>
> Intermittent reinforcement creates more persistent behavior because the organism is "used to" not always being rewarded, so absence of reward doesn't signal that reinforcement has ended.

## Question 5 - Punishment Caveats [application]

> A parent uses spanking to stop a child from hitting siblings. Research suggests several problems with this approach. List at least three.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> 1. **Suppresses, doesn't eliminate**: The hitting is suppressed in the parent's presence but may continue elsewhere (teaches discrimination)
> 2. **Models violence**: Physical punishment demonstrates aggressive behavior → child may learn aggression is an acceptable response (observational learning)
> 3. **Teaches fear of punisher, not understanding**: Child may fear the parent rather than learning WHY hitting is wrong
> 4. **Doesn't teach what TO do**: Tells the child what not to do, but doesn't guide them toward better behavior
> 5. **May create other problem behaviors**: The original behavior is reduced but may be replaced by other issues

## Question 6 - Shaping [application]

> A teacher wants to shape a student's behavior from "never raises hand" to "consistently raises hand before speaking." Describe a shaping strategy using successive approximations.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **Shaping via successive approximations**:
> 1. Reinforce any slight pause before speaking (approximation 1)
> 2. Once established, only reinforce when the student looks toward the teacher before speaking
> 3. Only reinforce when the student starts to lift their hand
> 4. Only reinforce when the hand is fully raised
> 5. Finally, only reinforce when the student waits to be called on
> Each step is reinforced until established, then the criterion is raised toward the final target behavior.

## Question 7 - Observational Learning [recall]

> What did Bandura's Bobo doll study (1963) demonstrate?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> Children who watched an adult **model** behave aggressively toward a Bobo doll subsequently showed **more aggression** toward the doll than children who watched a non-aggressive model or had no model. This demonstrated **observational learning** — behaviors can be acquired by watching others, without direct reinforcement or punishment of the observer. It also provided evidence that **vicarious reinforcement** can influence behavior.

## Question 8 - Latent Learning [recall]

> What is latent learning, and why did it challenge behaviorism?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **Latent learning** is learning that occurs without any obvious reinforcement and is not immediately demonstrated — it remains "hidden" until there is an incentive to use it. Classic example: rats who explored a maze without reward still learned its layout; when food was added, they navigated efficiently. This challenged behaviorism's claim that **reinforcement is necessary for learning** — organisms can learn through exploration and cognitive processing alone. This implies **mental representations (cognitive maps)** form even without reinforcement.

## Question 9 - Classical vs. Operant [recall]

> What is the fundamental difference between classical and operant conditioning in terms of the type of behavior involved?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> - **Classical conditioning**: involves **involuntary, reflexive** behavior. The organism has no control — it responds automatically to stimuli (e.g., salivating, flinching). The association is between two stimuli (NS + US).
> - **Operant conditioning**: involves **voluntary** behavior. The organism's actions operate on the environment and produce consequences. The association is between behavior and its outcome (reinforcement or punishment).

## Question 10 - Analysis [analysis]

> Slot machines pay out on an unpredictable, intermittent schedule. Using operant conditioning principles, explain why gambling behavior is so resistant to extinction, even after many losses.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> Slot machines use **intermittent (variable ratio) reinforcement** — reward is unpredictable and occurs after an *average* number of responses. This schedule produces: (1) **high response rates** — gamblers keep playing persistently; (2) **extreme resistance to extinction** — because payouts are unpredictable, many losses in a row don't signal that reinforcement has stopped; the gambler thinks "the next pull might win." This is the most powerful reinforcement schedule for maintaining behavior. The organism never knows when to stop trying.
