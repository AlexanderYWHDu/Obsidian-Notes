## Question 1 - Sleep Stages [recall]

> What brain waves characterize Non-REM Stage 3, and what are its primary functions?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **Delta waves** (slow waves) characterize N-REM 3. Primary functions include **physical restoration/repair** of body and brain, and **declarative memory consolidation** (particularly during hippocampal replay). It is more prominent in the **early** part of the night.

---

## Question 2 - Stage Comparison [recall]

> Compare REM sleep to Non-REM 3 sleep on: brain activity, body state, time of night, and primary function.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> | Feature | Non-REM 3 | REM |
> |---------|-----------|-----|
> | Brain activity | Delta waves (slow) | Mixed, like waking |
> | Body | Physically quiet | Paralyzed (motor suppressed) |
> | Time of night | **Early** (first cycles) | **Late** (last cycles) |
> | Primary function | Memory consolidation, physical repair | Cognitive function, dreaming |

## Question 3 - Circadian Rhythms [recall]

> What are circadian rhythms, and what does "social jet lag" mean?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **Circadian rhythms** are physical, mental, and behavioral changes following a 24-hour natural cycle (not just sleep/wake). **Social jet lag** is the mismatch between your biological circadian clock and social demands — e.g., sleeping late on weekends shifts your body clock, then Monday morning feels like jet lag.

---

## Question 4 - Circadian and Sports [application]

> A sports team flying from New York to Los Angeles (going west) is playing the next day. Should they be more or less worried about circadian disadvantage compared to a team flying east?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **More worried.** Going **west** is typically MORE detrimental to sports performance. When traveling west, your body clock is ahead of local time — your body thinks it's later than the local time, so evening games feel like they're happening even later at night from your internal clock perspective.

## Question 5 - Memory Consolidation [recall]

> Describe the Targeted Memory Reactivation (TMR) paradigm and what it demonstrated.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **Rudoy et al. (2009)**: Participants learned object-location pairs, each paired with a sound cue. During slow-wave sleep, some sounds were quietly replayed. Result: participants showed **better recall** for items whose sounds were played during sleep. This demonstrated that sleep actively promotes memory consolidation and that you can **selectively strengthen specific memories** during sleep.

## Question 6 - Sleep & Memory Views [recall]

> Contrast the initial (passive) view of why sleep benefits memory with the current (active) view.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **Initial view (passive)**: Sleep benefits memory because less incoming information means less interference — a passive benefit of reduced noise. **Current view (active)**: Sleep *actively* consolidates memories through hippocampal replay and reactivation of daytime experiences, forming new cortical associations. The hippocampus holds the event file and transfers it to the cortex during sleep consolidation. The active view is now better supported.

## Question 7 - Sleep Disorders [recall]

> A person suddenly falls asleep during a meeting at work. What disorder is most likely, and what is the appropriate treatment?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **Narcolepsy** — characterized by sudden, uncontrollable attacks of overwhelming sleepiness. Treatment: **stimulant medications** (to counteract the sleepiness).

---

## Question 8 - Parasomnias [recall]

> What is the key distinction between sleepwalking and REM sleep behavior disorder?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> - **Sleepwalking**: Occurs during **N-REM** sleep; person is NOT dreaming; typically no memory of the event; NOT dangerous to wake them; motor suppression is intact
> - **REM Sleep Behavior Disorder**: Occurs during **REM** sleep; person physically acts out their dreams (speaking, moving, hitting) because normal REM motor paralysis has failed

---

## Question 9 - Sleep & Student Performance [application]

> A student sleeps 8 hours Monday–Thursday but only 4–5 hours Friday–Sunday. Research suggests this pattern may be problematic even though their weekly average seems OK. Why?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **Sleep variability** — inconsistency in sleep timing and duration — is associated with lower GPA and higher anxiety, independent of average sleep amount. The body's circadian system relies on regularity; irregular schedules create "social jet lag" that impairs cognitive performance and mood even when total sleep hours seem adequate.

## Question 10 - Analysis [analysis]

> "Sleep is just passive downtime — the brain is resting." Evaluate this claim using three pieces of evidence from sleep research.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> This claim is **false**. Three pieces of evidence:
> 1. **Hippocampal replay (rats)**: Neural firing sequences from maze navigation are replayed during sleep — active memory processing.
> 2. **Targeted Memory Reactivation (humans)**: Playing sound cues during sleep selectively improves recall — sleep is not passive noise filtering.
> 3. **EEG data**: Distinct brain wave patterns (spindles, delta waves, REM mixed activity) demonstrate active, organized neural processes throughout sleep stages.
> Paller & Voss (2004): "Memories may not lie dormant during sleep, but rather may be receiving regular exercise."
