## Question 1 - Measuring Memory [recall]

> Distinguish between recall, recognition, and relearning as methods of measuring memory.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> - **Recall**: Retrieve information with no or minimal cues (e.g., short-answer test). Most demanding.
> - **Recognition**: Identify previously learned info from options (e.g., multiple choice). Easier — more cues.
> - **Relearning (savings method)**: Measure how much faster you re-learn something previously learned. Detects memory even when recall and recognition fail. Developed by Ebbinghaus.

## Question 2 - Ebbinghaus Forgetting Curve [recall]

> Describe the shape of Ebbinghaus's forgetting curve and what it reveals about memory.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> The curve shows **rapid initial forgetting**, followed by a plateau. Most forgetting happens within the first hours/days — information is lost quickly at first, then the remaining memories persist for much longer. This suggests that (1) review should happen soon after learning (before the rapid drop), and (2) material that survives the initial drop is relatively stable. Also shows memory is measurable via relearning even when recall is zero.

## Question 3 - Sensory Memory [recall]

> What is sensory memory, and what does it contain?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **Sensory memory** is the immediate, very brief recording of sensory information — a buffer that holds raw sensory data just slightly longer than the stimulus lasts (milliseconds to ~1-2 seconds). It has **large capacity** but decays nearly instantly. It holds unprocessed sensory information before selective attention filters what enters working memory.

## Question 4 - Sperling's Experiment [recall]

> Why did Sperling use a partial report method, and what did it prove?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> Sperling used partial report to test whether **all letters were briefly available** but decayed while being reported. In the whole report, only ~4.5 items were recalled — suggesting low capacity. But with partial report (told which row to report immediately), recall was 75–100%. This proved that **more than 4.5 items were available in sensory memory** — the whole report underestimated capacity because items decayed during the reporting process itself.

## Question 5 - STM vs. Working Memory [recall]

> What distinguishes working memory from the classic conception of short-term memory?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **Classic STM** (Miller) = passive storage buffer; holds ~7±2 items; simple digit span. **Working memory** = active mental workspace that involves both storage AND manipulation/processing of information. In a working memory task (e.g., remember numbers while solving equations), you must hold information AND perform operations on it simultaneously. WM is associated with higher cognitive functions like reasoning, comprehension, and problem-solving.

## Question 6 - Chunks [application]

> A student is trying to memorize this sequence: FBI CIA IRS NSA DEA. Why is this easier to remember than 15 random letters?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> Because the student knows these as **meaningful acronyms (chunks)** — each 3-letter sequence is processed as a single meaningful unit rather than 3 separate letters. The student is effectively storing only **5 chunks** rather than 15 items, well within working memory capacity. Chunking dramatically increases the effective capacity of working memory by packaging information into meaningful units.

## Question 7 - Method of Loci [recall]

> Describe the Method of Loci and explain why it is effective for increasing memory capacity.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> The **Method of Loci (Memory Palace)**: mentally "place" items along a familiar spatial path (your home, your body, etc.). At recall, mentally "walk" the path and "see" each item at its location. It is effective because it:
> 1. Exploits **spatial/episodic memory** (very robust in humans)
> 2. Provides distinct **retrieval cues** at each location
> 3. Forces **deep, elaborative encoding** (you have to visualize)
> 4. Creates a **structured retrieval path** (harder to skip items)

---

## Question 8 - Duration of STM [recall]

> Without rehearsal, how long does information typically last in working memory?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> Without rehearsal, information in working memory lasts approximately **20–30 seconds** before it decays. This is why you forget a phone number you just looked up if you get distracted before dialing. Maintenance rehearsal (repeating the information) can extend this, but it does not lead to strong long-term storage.

## Question 9 - Information Processing Model [recall]

> Describe the three stores in the information-processing model of memory including duration, capacity, and content.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> | Store | Duration | Capacity | Content |
> |-------|----------|----------|---------|
> | **Sensory Memory** | Milliseconds–1-2 sec | Large | Raw, unprocessed sensory data |
> | **Working/STM** | 20–30 sec (rehearsed: longer) | ~7±2 items / few chunks | Active information being processed |
> | **Long-Term Memory** | Years | Virtually unlimited | Knowledge, skills, experiences |

## Question 10 - Analysis [analysis]

> A student argues that they can memorize more by reading each flashcard 20 times rather than using any chunking or mnemonic strategy. Evaluate this claim using memory research.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> The claim is **weak**. Research shows:
> 1. **Maintenance rehearsal** (repetition) does not lead to strong long-term storage — it keeps information briefly in working memory but doesn't deeply encode it.
> 2. **Chunking** dramatically increases effective capacity and leverages existing long-term memory knowledge.
> 3. **Elaborative encoding** (mnemonics like Method of Loci) forces deeper, semantic processing → much stronger memory traces (levels of processing).
> 4. The relearning curve (Ebbinghaus) shows that passive repetition leads to rapid forgetting.
> Active engagement with meaning > passive repetition.
