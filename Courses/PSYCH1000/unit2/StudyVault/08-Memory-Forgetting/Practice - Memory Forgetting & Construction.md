## Question 1 - 7 Sins Classification [recall]

> Classify each sin as omission or commission: (a) persistence, (b) blocking, (c) suggestibility, (d) transience, (e) bias, (f) absentmindedness, (g) misattribution.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> - **Omission** (forgetting): (b) Blocking, (d) Transience, (f) Absentmindedness
> - **Commission** (distortion): (a) Persistence, (c) Suggestibility, (e) Bias, (g) Misattribution
> Note: Persistence is a "sin of remembering" — you remember something you'd rather forget — but is technically in the commission category.

## Question 2 - Interference Types [application]

> For each example, identify the type of interference: (a) A student learned French first, and now their Spanish vocabulary keeps intruding when they try to recall French. (b) A student learns Spanish after French, and now can't recall French vocabulary as well.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> - (a) **Proactive interference**: Old memory (French) interferes with retrieving the new memory (Spanish). Wait — actually the student learned French FIRST, and French intrudes when trying to recall French? Let me re-read. Student learned French, then Spanish, and Spanish intrudes when recalling French → **Retroactive interference** (new Spanish interferes with old French).
> - (b) Learned Spanish after French → can't recall French → **Retroactive interference** (new learning interferes with old)
>
> *Re-read (a)*: French intruding when trying to recall Spanish = **Proactive interference** (old French interferes with new Spanish recall).

## Question 3 - Tip of the Tongue [recall]

> What sin of memory is the tip-of-tongue (TOT) phenomenon an example of, and what does it tell us about memory availability vs. accessibility?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> TOT is an example of **Blocking** (3rd sin of omission). It demonstrates that more information is **available** in memory than is **accessible** at any given moment. The information is stored (you know you know it; you can often recall related features — first letter, syllable count) but temporarily cannot be retrieved. This shows that forgetting is often a **retrieval failure**, not necessarily a loss of the memory itself.

## Question 4 - Loftus & Palmer [recall]

> Describe the Loftus & Palmer (1974) study and its key finding.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> Participants watched a car accident film. Different groups received questions using either "smashed" or "hit." Key finding: **"Smashed" group** estimated higher speeds AND **32%** falsely recalled seeing broken glass (vs. 14% in "hit" group) — even though there was no broken glass in the film. This demonstrated the **misinformation effect**: post-event wording can implant false memories that alter people's recollection of the original event.

## Question 5 - Misattribution [application]

> A novelist is accused of plagiarism — a melody in her song closely matches one she heard years ago. She swears she "invented" it. What sin of memory explains this, and what is the specific term?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> This is **Misattribution** (4th sin), specifically **cryptomnesia** — forgetting that you heard something before and mistakenly believing you created it yourself. The memory of the melody is real, but the *source* is misattributed. She genuinely believes she composed it, but the source was something she heard and then "forgot" hearing.

## Question 6 - Hindsight Bias [recall]

> Define hindsight bias and explain which sin of memory it falls under.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **Hindsight bias**: After learning the outcome of an event, people believe they "knew it all along" — they retrospectively overestimate how predictable the outcome was. This falls under the **Bias** sin (6th sin, commission). Current knowledge distorts memory of past beliefs — people's recollection of their pre-outcome predictions shifts toward the actual outcome.

## Question 7 - Improving Memory: Testing Effect [recall]

> What did Roediger & Karpicke (2008) find about studying vs. testing?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> When comparing "Study-Study" vs. "Study-Test" conditions:
> - **Immediately after**: Study-Study performed slightly better
> - **After 1 week**: **Study-Test was significantly better** (~68% vs. ~54% recall)
> Conclusion: **Retrieval practice (testing)** produces better long-term memory than additional study time. This is the **testing effect** — the act of retrieving strengthens the memory trace more than re-encoding.

## Question 8 - Improving Memory: Spacing [application]

> A student has 6 days before an exam. Should they study 6 hours on Day 1, or 1 hour each day for 6 days? What does research say?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> Research strongly supports **studying 1 hour each day (spaced practice)**. Kornell & Bjork (2008) showed participants *thought* they'd do better with massed practice (cramming) but actually performed **better with spaced practice** on later tests. Spaced practice allows: (1) sleep consolidation between sessions, (2) effortful retrieval when revisiting material (forgetting + re-learning strengthens traces), (3) reduced interference.

## Question 9 - Rereading Trap [recall]

> Why is rereading an ineffective study strategy despite being so popular (~78% of students use it)?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> Rereading creates **familiarity** (the text becomes easier to process) which *feels like* mastery but is not. Three problems:
> 1. **Time-consuming**
> 2. **Doesn't create durable memory** — passive processing doesn't trigger strong encoding
> 3. **Illusion of mastery** — growing familiarity feels like knowledge, but familiarity ≠ ability to recall on a test
> The fluency of rereading is a form of "verisimilitude" — it feels true/familiar but doesn't reflect actual learning strength.

## Question 10 - Analysis [analysis]

> A defense lawyer argues that eyewitness testimony from a witness who was interviewed twice by police (with leading questions) should be excluded. Using memory research, build the strongest case for the defense.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> Strongest case using memory research:
> 1. **Suggestibility (Loftus & Palmer)**: Leading questions alter memory traces — "smashed" vs. "hit" changes recall. Police interviews using leading language contaminate memory.
> 2. **Misinformation effect**: Post-event information (from police questions, news, conversations) gets incorporated into the memory and cannot be fully separated out.
> 3. **Verisimilitude**: The witness's memory will *feel* true and vivid even if contaminated — juries may over-trust confident eyewitness testimony.
> 4. **255 wrongful convictions** as of 2026: 62% involved eyewitness misidentification — this is the single largest cause of wrongful convictions.
> 5. Memory is reconstructive, not a video recorder — each reconstruction changes the memory trace (reconsolidation), especially when suggestive questioning occurs.
