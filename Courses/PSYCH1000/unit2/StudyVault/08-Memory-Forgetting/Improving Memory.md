## Why Memory Needs Tricks

> [!important] The Core Challenge
> You need to trick your mind into thinking information is:
> 1. **Important** (deep processing signals importance)
> 2. **Not going to be accessible** later (forces effortful retrieval)

**Desirable Difficulties**: Training that is hard but not too hard → better long-term retention

---

## Strategy 1: Elaborative Encoding (Deep Processing)

```
SHALLOW ENCODING:          DEEP ENCODING:
• Rote repetition          • Connecting to existing knowledge
• Maintenance rehearsal    • Relating to personal experience
• "Is it uppercase?"       • "Does this fit in a sentence about my life?"

     ↓                              ↓
  Weaker trace               Stronger, more durable trace
```

- **Elaborative encoding** = adding more detail, making connections to other topics
- Example: Don't just reread; explain in your own words, link to examples

---

## Strategy 2: Testing Effect (Retrieval Practice)

> "Study, Study" vs. "Study, Test" — which produces better recall after 1 week?

- **Roediger & Karpicke (2008)**:
  - Study-Study condition: recall ~54% after 1 week
  - Study-Test condition: recall ~68% after 1 week
  - Testing produces **significantly better long-term retention**

```
IMMEDIATE RECALL:  Study-Study > Study-Test (testing hurts immediately)
1-WEEK RECALL:     Study-Test >> Study-Study (testing wins long-term)
```

> [!warning] The Rereading Trap
> ~78% of students use rereading as a major study strategy.
> But rereading: (1) is time-consuming, (2) doesn't create durable memory, (3) creates **illusion of mastery** — familiarity feels like knowledge but isn't.

---

## Strategy 3: Spaced Practice (Distributed Study)

- **Massed practice (cramming)**: all studying in one session
- **Spaced practice**: spreading study sessions over time

```
MASSED:  ████████████████ (one session, tonight)
          → Fast to learn, fast to forget

SPACED:  ████  ████  ████  (across days/weeks)
          → Slower to learn, much better long-term retention
```

- **Kornell & Bjork (2008)**: Students *think* they learn better with massed practice — but they **actually perform better** with spaced practice

> [!tip] Sports Analogy
> "Think about how ridiculous it would be to cram in a long weight-lifting session the night before you need strength... The same is true for your brain." — Doyle & Zakrajsek (2013)

---

## The Role of Forgetting in Learning

- **Forgetting can enhance learning** — if you forget some, then practice retrieving, you build stronger memory
- Spacing works partly because some forgetting occurs between sessions, making retrieval more effortful and therefore more strengthening

## Exam/Test Patterns

| Scenario/Keyword | Answer |
|-----------------|--------|
| Best study strategy for long-term recall | **Testing yourself + spaced practice** |
| Why is rereading ineffective? | Creates familiarity (not recall strength); illusion of mastery |
| Study-Test vs. Study-Study at 1 week | **Study-Test wins** |
| Cramming vs. spreading studying | **Spacing** produces better retention |
| Connecting new info to personal experience | **Elaborative encoding** |
| Why does spacing work? | Consolidation + effortful retrieval during gaps |
