## The Two-Track Mind (Dual Processing)

```
INCOMING INFORMATION
        │
        ▼
┌───────────────────────────────────┐
│  UNCONSCIOUS PROCESSING           │
│  • Many aspects simultaneously    │
│  • Brain's natural default mode   │
│  • Operates without awareness     │
└───────────────────────────────────┘
        │  (selected subset only)
        ▼
┌───────────────────────────────────┐
│  CONSCIOUS PROCESSING             │
│  • One stimulus at a time         │
│  • Selective attention required   │
└───────────────────────────────────┘
```

- We consciously attend to a **very limited portion** of incoming information
- Everything else is processed **unconsciously** — this is adaptive (prevents cognitive overload)

## Types of Attention

### Exogenous Attention
- **External** stimuli capture your attention automatically
- Examples: phone notification, something flying toward your head, flickering lights, jump scares

### Endogenous Attention
- You **voluntarily control** your attention
- Examples: focusing on studying, paying attention to a lecture, blocking out noise

> [!tip] William James (1890)
> "Attention is the taking possession by the mind, in clear and vivid form, of one out of what seem several simultaneously possible objects..."

## Multitasking

> [!important] Core Finding
> We are bad at multitasking AND bad at knowing we are bad at multitasking.

- **Multitasking** = engaging with two tasks or streams of information simultaneously
- Unless one task is **highly automatized**, we are really **task-switching** (not parallel processing)
- **Frequent multitaskers** actually multitask *worse* than infrequent multitaskers
- Real-world evidence:
  - Final exam score & nonacademic internet use during class: **r = −0.25**
  - Median time on other sites in 100-min class: **37 minutes**
  - Laptop use harms even *nearby students* who see others' screens (Sana et al., 2013)

### Supertaskers
- Roughly **2.5%** of people can truly multitask (driving simulator + working memory task with no impairment)
- Statistically, most people in a class of 200 cannot do this

## Exam/Test Patterns

| Scenario/Keyword | Answer |
|-----------------|--------|
| You "focus" on lecture despite noise | **Endogenous attention** |
| Your phone buzzes and you look | **Exogenous attention** |
| Doing two things at once (one automatized) | Technically possible: one is on **autopilot** |
| Heavy multitasker vs. light multitasker: who's better? | **Light multitasker** is better |
| What is actually happening during "multitasking"? | **Task-switching** |
