## Question 1 - Disorder Identification [recall]

> A patient can accurately reach for and grasp objects placed on the left side of their visual field, but insists they cannot see anything. Which disorder best explains this?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **Blindsight.** Damage to the primary visual cortex destroys conscious visual experience, but subcortical visual pathways remain intact, allowing perception-guided behavior without consciousness. The patient acts on what they cannot consciously "see."

---

## Question 2 - Disorder Identification [recall]

> After a stroke affecting his right parietal lobe, a patient only draws the right half of objects and ignores food on the left side of his plate. His vision test shows no field cut. What condition does he have, and why is it NOT a vision problem?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **Unilateral neglect.** The right parietal lobe is responsible for attending to the left side of space. Damage causes an *attentional* failure — the left side of space is simply not available for conscious awareness, even though the visual system itself is intact.

## Question 3 - Clinical Cases [recall]

> Match each clinical case to the correct disorder: (a) Terri Schiavo, (b) Jean-Dominique Bauby, (c) Jan Grzebski

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> - **(a) Terri Schiavo** → Persistent Vegetative State (wakefulness without awareness)
> - **(b) Jean-Dominique Bauby** → Locked-in Syndrome (full consciousness, communicated by blinking)
> - **(c) Jan Grzebski** → Minimally Conscious State (some fluctuating awareness)

## Question 4 - Dual Processing [recall]

> What does the "two-track mind" mean, and why is it adaptive?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> The two-track mind refers to **dual processing**: the brain simultaneously runs unconscious processing (handling many things at once) and conscious processing (focused on one thing). This is adaptive because it prevents cognitive overload — we can't consciously process the enormous amount of sensory information arriving every second, so most is handled unconsciously.

## Question 5 - Attention Types [application]

> A student is studying when their phone buzzes. They look at it immediately. Then they force themselves to put it away and focus back on their notes. Identify the type of attention in each case.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> - Looking at the buzzing phone → **Exogenous attention** (external stimulus captures attention involuntarily)
> - Forcing themselves to return to notes → **Endogenous attention** (voluntarily directed focus)

---

## Question 6 - Multitasking [recall]

> Research finds that heavy multitaskers actually multitask worse than light multitaskers. What does this suggest about people who frequently divide their attention?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> It suggests that **frequent multitasking degrades the ability to multitask** — habitually dividing attention may impair the executive control needed to manage tasks. Most "multitasking" is actually **task-switching**, and heavy multitaskers become worse at filtering out irrelevant information.

---

## Question 7 - Inattentional Blindness [application]

> During a busy police training run, a researcher staged a fight visible for 30 seconds. Only 56% of officers noticed. What does this demonstrate, and what is its practical implication?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> This demonstrates **inattentional blindness** — when attention is directed elsewhere (focused on running), visible events can go completely unnoticed. Practical implication: eyewitness accounts by people who were focused on another task may be unreliable, and training cannot guarantee that people will notice unexpected events.

---

## Question 8 - Multitasking Research [recall]

> In Ravizza et al. (2017), what was the correlation between nonacademic internet use during class and final exam scores?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **r = −0.25** — a negative correlation. More nonacademic internet use during class was associated with lower exam scores, and this relationship held for both high and low ACT scorers. Additionally, the median time spent on other sites during 100 minutes of class was **37 minutes**.

---

## Question 9 - Change Blindness [recall]

> What is change blindness, and how is it related to inattentional blindness?

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> **Change blindness** is the failure to notice changes in a visual scene (e.g., actor switching between film cuts). It is a **subtype of inattentional blindness** — both arise from the selective, limited nature of attention. When attention is not specifically directed to a changing element, the change goes unnoticed.

---

## Question 10 - Analysis [analysis]

> A professor claims: "If students just paid more attention, they would notice everything important." Use evidence from the dual processing model and research on inattentional blindness to evaluate this claim.

> [!answer]- 정답 보기
> The claim is **overly simplistic**. The dual processing model shows that conscious attention is inherently selective and limited — we cannot consciously attend to everything simultaneously. Research (Simons, Ravizza) shows that even trained professionals (police officers) miss visible events when attention is elsewhere. Moreover, ~2.5% of people are supertaskers; the vast majority cannot truly divide attention. The adaptive function of selective attention is to allow efficient processing of what matters most, at the cost of missing other things.
