## Part A — Consciousness & Attention (Ch. 8)

**Q1.** A patient has normal sleep-wake cycles and full awareness, but can only communicate through eye movements. This condition is best described as:

- A) Vegetative State
- B) Minimally Conscious State
- C) Locked-in Syndrome
- D) Coma

> [!check]- Answer
> **C) Locked-in Syndrome**
> Locked-in Syndrome is notable because it is *not* a disorder of consciousness — the patient has full awareness and normal sleep-wake cycles, but is paralyzed except for eye movements.

---

**Q2.** A patient with damage to the right parietal lobe draws only the right side of their face in a self-portrait. This is an example of:

- A) Blindsight
- B) Unilateral Neglect
- C) Inattentional Blindness
- D) Change Blindness

> [!check]- Answer
> **B) Unilateral Neglect**
> Unilateral Neglect results from right parietal lobe injury. It is an impairment of *attention*, not vision — patients cannot attend to the left side of space.

---

**Q3.** Your phone buzzes on the desk and you immediately look at it. This is an example of:

- A) Endogenous Attention
- B) Unconscious Processing
- C) Exogenous Attention
- D) Selective Filtering

> [!check]- Answer
> **C) Exogenous Attention**
> Exogenous attention is externally triggered and automatic. Endogenous attention is internally directed (e.g., choosing to focus on studying).

---

**Q4.** Which of the following best describes "Blindsight"?

- A) Inability to attend to one side of visual space
- B) Perception without conscious awareness, caused by primary visual cortex damage
- C) Failure to notice changes in a visual scene
- D) Inability to form new visual memories

> [!check]- Answer
> **B) Perception without conscious awareness, caused by primary visual cortex damage**
> Blindsight patients can respond to visual stimuli (e.g., reaching for objects) but have no conscious experience of "seeing" them.

## Part B — Sleep (Ch. 9)

**Q5.** Which sleep stage is characterized by delta waves and is critical for memory consolidation and muscle development?

- A) REM Sleep
- B) NREM-1
- C) NREM-2
- D) NREM-3 (Slow-wave sleep)

> [!check]- Answer
> **D) NREM-3 (Slow-wave sleep)**
> NREM-3 produces delta waves and is the deepest stage of sleep. It is critical for physiological repair and memory consolidation.

---

**Q6.** During REM sleep, which of the following does NOT occur?

- A) Dreaming
- B) Brain activity similar to waking
- C) Motor paralysis
- D) Delta wave production

> [!check]- Answer
> **D) Delta wave production**
> Delta waves occur in NREM-3 (slow-wave sleep), not REM. REM features near-waking brain activity, dreaming, rapid eye movements, and motor paralysis.

---

**Q7.** According to research, which factor is the MOST significant predictor of poor academic performance?

- A) Homesickness
- B) Roommate conflicts
- C) Sleep difficulty
- D) Chronic health problems

> [!check]- Answer
> **C) Sleep difficulty**
> Research indicates that sleep difficulty outranks homesickness, roommate issues, and chronic health problems as a predictor of poor academic performance.

---

**Q8.** The "hippocampal replay" model of sleep and memory suggests that:

- A) Sleep prevents new information from interfering with old memories
- B) The hippocampus permanently stores all long-term memories
- C) During sleep, the brain reactivates associations and transfers them from hippocampus to cortex
- D) REM sleep is solely responsible for all memory consolidation

> [!check]- Answer
> **C) During sleep, the brain reactivates associations and transfers them from hippocampus to cortex**
> The modern view holds that sleep *actively* strengthens memories through hippocampal replay, eventually making the cortex the permanent store without needing the hippocampus.

---

**Q9.** A professional basketball team flies from Los Angeles to New York for an evening game. According to circadian rhythm research, this travel:

- A) Has no effect on performance
- B) Is beneficial because eastward travel aligns with the body clock
- C) Is detrimental because traveling east forces the body to perform earlier than its internal clock
- D) Only affects sleep quality, not athletic performance

> [!check]- Answer
> **C) Is detrimental because traveling east forces the body to perform earlier than its internal clock**
> Traveling east is generally more detrimental than traveling west for athletic performance due to circadian disadvantage.

---

**Q10.** Kenneth Parks was acquitted of murder using which legal defense?

- A) Insanity due to narcolepsy
- B) Automatism (homicidal sleepwalking)
- C) Dissociative identity disorder
- D) Hypnosis-induced amnesia

> [!check]- Answer
> **B) Automatism (homicidal sleepwalking)**
> Parks argued he was unaware of his actions due to sleepwalking — a parasomnia. This is an example of the "automatism" legal defense.

## Part C — Drugs & Substance Use (Ch. 10)

**Q11.** Which category of psychoactive drugs is correctly matched with its effects?

- A) Stimulants — euphoria and intense pain relief
- B) Depressants — distorted perceptions and hallucinations
- C) Opioids — alertness and appetite suppression
- D) Hallucinogens — distorted perceptions and altered sensory experiences

> [!check]- Answer
> **D) Hallucinogens — distorted perceptions and altered sensory experiences**
> Stimulants = alertness; Depressants = slowed body functions; Opioids = euphoria/pain relief; Hallucinogens = distorted perceptions.

---

**Q12.** A person needs increasingly larger doses of a drug to feel the same effect. This is called:

- A) Withdrawal
- B) Tolerance
- C) Habituation
- D) Sensitization

> [!check]- Answer
> **B) Tolerance**
> Tolerance = needing more of a substance for the same effect. Withdrawal = distress upon stopping. Both are "Drug Action" criteria for SUD diagnosis.

## Part D — Learning (Ch. 21–23)

**Q13.** In Pavlov's experiment, the bell (after conditioning) is the:

- A) Unconditioned Stimulus (US)
- B) Unconditioned Response (UR)
- C) Conditioned Stimulus (CS)
- D) Neutral Stimulus (NS)

> [!check]- Answer
> **C) Conditioned Stimulus (CS)**
> Before conditioning, the bell is a Neutral Stimulus (NS). After being repeatedly paired with food (US), it becomes a Conditioned Stimulus (CS) that elicits salivation on its own.

---

**Q14.** After extinction, a dog that had been conditioned to salivate at a bell stops responding. A week later, the bell is rung again and the dog salivates briefly. This is called:

- A) Generalization
- B) Discrimination
- C) Re-acquisition
- D) Spontaneous Recovery

> [!check]- Answer
> **D) Spontaneous Recovery**
> Spontaneous Recovery is the reappearance of an extinguished CR after a rest period. It shows that extinction does not erase the original learning.

---

**Q15.** A child gets a time-out (removal of iPad privileges) for hitting a sibling. This is an example of:

- A) Positive Punishment
- B) Negative Punishment
- C) Negative Reinforcement
- D) Positive Reinforcement

> [!check]- Answer
> **B) Negative Punishment**
> Negative Punishment = *removing* a pleasant stimulus to *decrease* a behavior. The iPad (pleasant stimulus) is taken away to reduce hitting.

---

**Q16.** A car seatbelt alarm stops beeping once you buckle up. Buckling the seatbelt is maintained by:

- A) Positive Reinforcement
- B) Positive Punishment
- C) Negative Reinforcement
- D) Negative Punishment

> [!check]- Answer
> **C) Negative Reinforcement**
> Negative Reinforcement = *removing* an unpleasant stimulus (the beeping) to *increase* a behavior (buckling up). Note: "Negative" refers to removal, not something bad.

---

**Q17.** Conditioned Taste Aversion differs from typical classical conditioning because:

- A) It requires hundreds of trials to form
- B) It can form after a single pairing and with a long delay between CS and US
- C) It only works with visual stimuli
- D) It cannot be extinguished

> [!check]- Answer
> **B) It can form after a single pairing and with a long delay between CS and US**
> This violates the typical rules of conditioning (multiple trials, short CS-US interval), demonstrating the role of biological preparedness.

---

**Q18.** Rats explored a maze with no reward for 10 days. On day 11, food was introduced, and they immediately showed a dramatic drop in errors. This demonstrates:

- A) Operant conditioning
- B) Spontaneous recovery
- C) Latent Learning
- D) Behavioral Shaping

> [!check]- Answer
> **C) Latent Learning**
> Latent Learning shows that cognitive maps can form without reinforcement. This challenged pure behaviorism by demonstrating the role of mental processes.

---

**Q19.** Which statement about punishment is TRUE?

- A) Punishment eliminates the underlying behavior permanently
- B) Punishment tells an organism what TO do
- C) Punishment suppresses behavior but does not eliminate it; the behavior may return in different contexts
- D) Punishment is more effective than reinforcement for teaching new skills

> [!check]- Answer
> **C) Punishment suppresses behavior but does not eliminate it; the behavior may return in different contexts**
> Punishment teaches discrimination (stop in this context), can model aggression, and only says "don't do this" — not "do this instead."

## Part E — Memory (Ch. 24–26)

**Q20.** George Miller's "Magical Number" refers to the capacity of:

- A) Sensory Memory (~4.5 items)
- B) Short-Term / Working Memory (7 ± 2 chunks)
- C) Long-Term Memory (unlimited)
- D) Implicit Memory (7 categories)

> [!check]- Answer
> **B) Short-Term / Working Memory (7 ± 2 chunks)**
> Miller (1956) found STM capacity is approximately 7 ± 2 *chunks* — not individual items. Chunking can dramatically expand how much information fits in this limited space.

---

**Q21.** You remember the first and last words of a list best, but forget the middle. This is called the:

- A) Encoding Specificity Effect
- B) Serial Position Effect (Primacy + Recency)
- C) Levels of Processing Effect
- D) Context-Dependent Memory Effect

> [!check]- Answer
> **B) Serial Position Effect (Primacy + Recency)**
> Primacy Effect = better recall for early items (linked to LTM). Recency Effect = better recall for recent items (linked to WM). The middle items are most poorly recalled.

---

**Q22.** Patient HM had his hippocampus removed and could no longer form new long-term memories. However, he showed improvement on a mirror-drawing task over repeated sessions (without remembering doing it). This preserved ability reflects:

- A) Semantic Memory
- B) Episodic Memory
- C) Implicit / Procedural Memory
- D) Working Memory

> [!check]- Answer
> **C) Implicit / Procedural Memory**
> Procedural (implicit) memory is stored in the cerebellum/basal ganglia, not the hippocampus. HM's case proved the double dissociation between explicit and implicit memory systems.

---

**Q23.** You study for an exam in the same room where you will take it. Your performance improves due to matching environmental cues. This is called:

- A) State-Dependent Memory
- B) Context-Dependent Memory
- C) Encoding Specificity
- D) Elaborative Encoding

> [!check]- Answer
> **B) Context-Dependent Memory**
> Context-Dependent Memory = external environmental cues at retrieval match those at encoding → better recall. State-Dependent Memory refers to *internal* states (mood, sobriety).

---

**Q24.** In the Loftus & Palmer (1974) study, participants who heard the word "smashed" (vs. "hit") to describe a car accident later:

- A) Recalled the accident more accurately
- B) Estimated lower speeds and forgot details
- C) Estimated higher speeds and falsely recalled broken glass
- D) Showed no difference in recall

> [!check]- Answer
> **C) Estimated higher speeds and falsely recalled broken glass**
> This demonstrates **Suggestibility** — one of the Seven Sins of Memory. Leading questions can implant false details into a person's memory.

---

**Q25.** Hyde & Jenkins (1973) found that participants who processed words *deeply* (semantically) without being told to remember them recalled as much as those who *intentionally* tried to memorize. This shows:

- A) Intention to learn is more important than depth of processing
- B) Depth of processing matters more than the intention to remember
- C) Shallow processing is sufficient for long-term retention
- D) Rereading is the most effective study strategy

> [!check]- Answer
> **B) Depth of processing matters more than the intention to remember**
> Deep semantic processing (~0.69 recall) matched intentional memorization, while shallow processing yielded far lower recall (~0.39–0.43).

---

**Q26.** Which of the following is a "Sin of Omission" (a type of forgetting)?

- A) Suggestibility
- B) Misattribution
- C) Persistence
- D) Blocking

> [!check]- Answer
> **D) Blocking**
> Sins of Omission = Transience, Absentmindedness, Blocking (all involve failing to retrieve). Suggestibility and Misattribution are Sins of Commission (distortion). Persistence is the Sin of Remembering.

---

**Q27.** According to the Innocence Project data (as of 2026), what percentage of wrongful convictions involved eyewitness misidentification?

- A) 29%
- B) 47%
- C) 62%
- D) 78%

> [!check]- Answer
> **C) 62%**
> 62% involved eyewitness misidentification; 29% involved false confessions. This highlights how unreliable and malleable human memory can be in legal contexts.

## Part F — Thinking (Ch. 27)

**Q28.** An "Aha!" moment of sudden insight is associated with a burst of activity in which brain region?

- A) Left frontal lobe
- B) Right temporal lobe
- C) Hippocampus
- D) Amygdala

> [!check]- Answer
> **B) Right temporal lobe**
> Insight — the sudden realization of a solution without a step-by-step strategy — is linked to activity in the right temporal lobe.

---

**Q29.** In "Opt-Out" countries for organ donation (like Austria), consent rates are ~99.98%, compared to ~12% in "Opt-In" countries (like Germany). This difference is best explained by:

- A) Confirmation Bias
- B) Availability Heuristic
- C) Framing Effects and Behavioral Nudging
- D) Mental Set

> [!check]- Answer
> **C) Framing Effects and Behavioral Nudging**
> The default option (opt-out vs. opt-in) frames the same choice differently, dramatically changing behavior — a classic demonstration of framing effects.

---

**Q30.** The tendency to judge how common shark attacks are based on how easily news stories about them come to mind is an example of:

- A) Confirmation Bias
- B) The Availability Heuristic
- C) Framing Effect
- D) Fixation

> [!check]- Answer
> **B) The Availability Heuristic**
> The Availability Heuristic = judging frequency/probability by ease of recall. Vivid, memorable events (like shark attacks) feel more common than they statistically are.

## Part G — Language & Intelligence (Ch. 28 + Intelligence)

**Q31.** Whorf's Linguistic Determinism claims that language *determines* thought. The modern, accepted view is:

- A) Language has no influence on thought
- B) Language *influences* thought (Linguistic Relativism)
- C) Thought is entirely independent of language
- D) Language and thought develop completely separately

> [!check]- Answer
> **B) Language *influences* thought (Linguistic Relativism)**
> Linguistic Determinism (strong form) is considered a myth. Linguistic Relativism — that language shapes but does not determine thought — is the accepted moderate position.

---

**Q32.** At approximately what age do infants produce speech sounds that begin to resemble their household language specifically (rather than all languages)?

- A) 4 months
- B) 9 months
- C) 10 months
- D) 12 months

> [!check]- Answer
> **C) 10 months**
> At 4 months: babbling (general). At 9 months: utterances are language-general. At **10 months**: babbling narrows to resemble the household language. At 12 months: first single words.

---

**Q33.** In CHC Theory, which type of intelligence tends to *decline* with age?

- A) Crystallized Intelligence
- B) Fluid Intelligence
- C) Analytical Intelligence
- D) Practical Intelligence

> [!check]- Answer
> **B) Fluid Intelligence**
> Fluid Intelligence (abstract/novel reasoning) typically declines with age. Crystallized Intelligence (accumulated knowledge/vocabulary) remains stable or increases.

---

**Q34.** The Flynn Effect refers to:

- A) The decline of IQ scores since the 1980s
- B) The increase in IQ scores by ~2–3 points per decade throughout the 20th century
- C) The stability of IQ scores across a lifetime
- D) The gap in IQ scores between different socioeconomic groups

> [!check]- Answer
> **B) The increase in IQ scores by ~2–3 points per decade throughout the 20th century**
> The Flynn Effect documents rising IQ scores across generations, attributed to better nutrition, education, and familiarity with abstract testing. Recent data suggests the trend may be slowing.

---

**Q35.** The "Marshmallow Test" is used to demonstrate the predictive power of:

- A) IQ on academic achievement
- B) Crystallized intelligence on long-term success
- C) Self-control/self-discipline on life outcomes
- D) Gardner's interpersonal intelligence

> [!check]- Answer
> **C) Self-control/self-discipline on life outcomes**
> 4-year-olds who delayed gratification (didn't eat the marshmallow) later showed higher SAT scores, better grades, and lower BMIs — suggesting self-control can outpredict IQ.
