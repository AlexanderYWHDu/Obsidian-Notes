---
## Disorders of Visual Consciousness

Visual consciousness disorders highlight the separation between "seeing" (perception) and "knowing" (conscious awareness).

- **Blindsight:** Caused by damage to the primary visual cortex. Individuals experience perception without consciousness, often attributing "seeing-like actions" to mere "feelings" or intuition.
- **Unilateral Neglect:** Typically resulting from injury to the right parietal lobe. This is an impairment of ==_attention_, not vision==. Patients are unable to attend to the left side of space, effectively removing it from their conscious awareness. This is illustrated by self-portraits made during recovery where patients only draw the right side of their faces.
---
## The Mechanics of Attention and the Dual-Track Mind

The human brain functions as a "two-track mind," utilizing both conscious and unconscious processing simultaneously.

### Dual Processing Tracks

1. **Conscious Processing:** Focusing awareness on a single particular stimulus. This is a limited resource.
2. **Unconscious Processing:** The brain’s natural mode for most functions, processing many aspects of a problem simultaneously outside of awareness. This is adaptive, as it prevents the conscious mind from being overwhelmed by thousands of stimuli.

### Selective Attention

Attention is the withdrawal from some things in order to deal effectively with others. It is often compared to a flashlight beam.

- **Exogenous Attention:** External stimuli that capture attention automatically (e.g., a phone notification or a jump scare).
- **Endogenous Attention:** Internally controlled focus (e.g., studying or actively shutting out distracting noises).

### Inattentional and Change Blindness

Because attention is limited, humans are functionally blind to non-attended information.

- **Inattentional Blindness:** Failing to see visible objects when attention is directed elsewhere.
- **Multi-tasking:** Humans are generally poor at multi-tasking and, crucially, are unaware of how poorly they perform when attempting to process two streams of information simultaneously.

# 9. Sleep

## The Biological Architecture of Sleep

Sleep is a natural, periodic loss of consciousness distinct from coma or anesthesia. It is regulated by **circadian rhythms**, 24-hour cycles of physical and mental changes. The sleep/wake cycle is regulated by **melatonin** production, less light leads to less melatonin produced, thus makes people feel sleepy.

---
## Sleep Stages and Cycles

A typical night consists of four to five 90-minute cycles.

| Stage                          | Wave Type / Characteristics      | Notable Features                                                                                                                                                                         |
| ------------------------------ | -------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Awake/Alert**                | Beta Waves                       | Normal waking state.                                                                                                                                                                     |
| **Awake/Drowsy**               | Alpha Waves                      | Relaxed state before sleep onset.                                                                                                                                                        |
| **NREM-1**                     | N1                               | Light sleep; may include hypnagogic sensations.                                                                                                                                          |
| **NREM-2**                     | N2                               | Deeper sleep than N1.                                                                                                                                                                    |
| **NREM-3**~==Slow-wave sleep== | Delta Waves                      | critical for memory and muscle development.                                                                                                                                              |
| **REM Sleep**                  | Brain activity similar to waking | Rapid/irregular breathing; heart rate rises; dreaming occurs; motor paralysis(**“sleep paralysis”--May linger into waking**); brain activity closest to waking state; difficult to wake. |
REM + slow-wave sleep are critical to cognitive functioning
### Life Span and Regulation

- **Developmental Changes:** REM sleep drops markedly across life. As humans age, the total time spent in sleep decreases, and the sleep architecture becomes more fragmented.
- ![[Pasted image 20260320162626.png]]
- **Regulation:** The suprachiasmatic nucleus in the brain responds to light, signaling the pineal gland to suppress melatonin production during the day.
- **Chronotypes:** Individuals often fall into categories of "Larks" (morning people) or "Owls" (evening people).
---
## The Impact of Sleep on Performance and Health

Sleep is not a passive state but an active period of repair and cognitive reinforcement.

### Cognitive and Academic Consequences

Research indicates that sleep difficulty is a more significant predictor of poor academic performance than homesickness, roommate issues, or chronic health problems.

- **GPA:** Sleep variability (inconsistent sleep/wake times) is a significant negative predictor of GPA.
- **Well-being:** Average weekly sleep quantity correlates positively with happiness and negatively with anxiety.
- **Athletic Performance:** Travel across time zones creates "circadian disadvantage." Traveling east is generally more detrimental to sports performance than traveling west.

### The Functions of Sleep

1. **Protection:** Staying out of harm's way during dangerous times (e.g., darkness).
2. **Repair and Restoration:** Repairing damaged neurons, sweeping away toxic waste, and secreting growth hormones for muscle development.
3. **Creative Thinking:** Promoting insight and problem-solving.
4. **Memory Consolidation:** Sleep actively promotes the process of turning encoded information into durable long-term memories.

### Memory Consolidation Mechanism

During sleep, the brain "replays" and reactivates associations learned during the day.

- **Hippocampus:** Binds information into event indexes; critical for initial storage.
- **Cortex:** During storage and repeated retrieval during sleep, new associative connections are formed in the cortex until the hippocampus is no longer necessary for retrieval.
- **Targeted Memory Reactivation:** Experiments (e.g., Rudoy et al., 2009) show that cuing memories during sleep (using sounds associated with learning) can reduce the rate of forgetting.

### Sleep Stages and Memory

Sleep is divided into distinct stages, each serving specific functions:

- **Slow-wave Sleep:** Occurs more frequently in the earlier part of the night. It is critical for memory reconsolidation and physiological health.
- **REM (Rapid Eye Movement) Sleep:** Increases as the night progresses. It is vital for cognitive functions and is the primary stage for dreaming.

While initial views suggested sleep protected memory by reducing interference, modern perspectives argue that sleep actively strengthens associations through "hippocampal replay" and "targeted memory reactivation." During sleep, the brain replays and reactivates information learned during the day, forming new associative connections in the cortex.

### Sleep Disruptions and Disorders

The failure to maintain healthy sleep patterns has significant implications for performance and safety.

- **Circadian Disadvantages:** In professional sports (NBA, NHL, NFL), teams traveling westward often experience a "circadian disadvantage" during evening games because playing late at night according to their internal clocks is difficult.
- **Safety Risks:** Data indicates a sharp increase in accidents following the Spring time change (losing an hour of sleep) and a decrease in accidents after the Fall change (gaining an hour).
- **Sleep Disorders:**
    - **Insomnia:** Persistent difficulty falling or staying asleep.
    - **Narcolepsy:** Sudden, overwhelming attacks of sleepiness.
    - **Parasomnias:** Abnormal behaviors during sleep, including sleepwalking (somnambulism) and REM sleep behavior disorder (enacting dreams).
    - **Automatism:** A legal defense used in cases such as Kenneth Parks, who was acquitted of murder due to "homicidal sleepwalking," arguing he was unaware of his actions.

---
## Sleep Deprivation and Hygiene

The lack of sleep leads to a "shocking" number of negative outcomes, including immune suppression, inflammation, increased stress hormones, poor decision-making, and increased risk of accidents or dementia.

Strategies for Improving Sleep Quality

- **Maintain a regular schedule:** Go to bed and wake up at the same time daily.
- **Optimize the environment:** Keep the room cool, dark, and quiet.
- **Avoid Stimulants:** Limit caffeine, alcohol, and nicotine, especially late in the day.
- **Manage Light Exposure:** Avoid screen time before bed and minimize stimulation.
- **Physical Activity:** Exercise regularly, but avoid intense activity in the late evening.
- **Behavioral Habits:** Do not stay in bed while awake; avoid late afternoon naps and food/drink immediately before bed.

# 10. Drugs & substance use disorder

## Altered States of Consciousness

Beyond sleep, consciousness can be altered through intentional practices or chemical intervention.

- **Hypnosis:** Involves a state of focused attention and enhanced suggestibility. Clinically, it is used for pain reduction, weight loss, and smoking cessation.
- **Meditation:** Provides measurable benefits, including reduced stress and blood pressure, improved attention, and changes in brain activity, specifically for patients with depression.

### Psychoactive Drugs and Substance Use Disorder (SUD)

Psychoactive drugs alter perceptions and moods by changing brain chemistry.

|Drug Type|Examples|Effects|
|---|---|---|
|**Depressants**|Alcohol, Tranquilizers|Slow body functions, memory disruption, disinhibition.|
|**Opioids**|Heroin, Codeine|Euphoria, intense pain relief.|
|**Stimulants**|Nicotine, Methamphetamine|Alertness, appetite suppression, excited neural activity.|
|**Hallucinogens**|LSD, Marijuana|Distorted perceptions, altered sensory experiences.|

---
## Substance Use Disorder Indicators

SUD is diagnosed based on a spectrum of indicators across four categories:

1. **Diminished Control:** Using more than intended, craving the substance, and failed attempts to regulate use.
2. **Social Functioning:** Use disrupting work or home commitments and continued use despite social problems.
3. **Hazardous Use:** Continued use despite physical or psychological danger.
4. **Drug Action:** Experiencing **tolerance** (needing larger doses for the same effect) and **withdrawal** (distress upon discontinuation).
---
## Addictive Technology

Internal communications from major tech firms (Meta, YouTube) compare social media features like "infinite scroll" and algorithmic recommendations to "casinos" or "drugs." Legal challenges argue these products are designed to cause compulsive use and contribute to mental health issues like anxiety and depression.

# 21-23. Learning

Learning is the process of acquiring new information or behaviors through experience.
We learn 
• To expect and prepare for significant events (classical conditioning) 
• To repeat acts that bring rewards, avoid acts that bring unwanted results (operant conditioning) 
We learn new behaviors 
• By observing events and people, and through language 
• We learn things we have neither experienced nor observed (cognitive learning)

---
## Classical Conditioning

Classical (Pavlovian) conditioning involves learning to expect and prepare for significant events by linking two or more stimuli.

- **Neutral Stimulus (NS):** Elicits no response before conditioning.
- **Unconditioned Stimulus (US):** Naturally and automatically triggers a response (e.g., food).
- **Unconditioned Response (UR):** A naturally occurring reaction (e.g., salivating).
- **Conditioned Stimulus (CS):** A previously neutral stimulus that, after association with a US, triggers a response.
- **Conditioned Response (CR):** A learned response to a previously neutral stimulus.
- ![[Pasted image 20260320164738.png]]
- ![[Pasted image 20260320164712.png|674]]

### Stages of Conditioning

- **Acquisition:** The initial stage where the NS and US are linked. This works best when the NS precedes the US closely in time.
- **Extinction:** The diminishing of a CR when the US no longer follows the CS.
- **Spontaneous Recovery:** The reappearance of an extinguished CR after a pause.
- ![[Pasted image 20260320165153.png]]
- **Generalization:** The tendency for stimuli similar to the CS to elicit similar responses (e.g., Little Albert fearing all white, fluffy objects after being conditioned to fear a white rat).
- **Discrimination:** The learned ability to distinguish between a CS and other irrelevant stimuli.

### Empirical Examples and Applications

- **Little Albert:** Researchers conditioned a fear response in an infant by pairing a white rat (NS) with a loud noise (US). Eventually, the rat alone (CS) triggered fear behavior (CR).
-  **Self-Conditioning:** An individual can classically condition themselves by pairing a strong, naturally arising response (like salivating to lemonade powder) with a neutral stimulus (like a specific song) over many trials.
- **Preparedness**: an animal’s capacity for conditioning is limited by biological constraints – for mammals, taste is a good signal of safety to eat (on the other hand - birds use sight) So, taste/sickness easy to associate

### Constraints on acquisition
**Conditioned Taste Aversion:** This occurs when a specific food (NS) is paired with nausea (US), often due to drugs or illness. Unlike other forms of conditioning, taste aversion can occur after a single pairing and even if several hours elapse between the stimulus and the response.
    - _Application:_ Children undergoing chemotherapy may develop an aversion to "scapegoat" foods (like hospital ice cream) if they consume them before treatment-induced nausea.

---
## Operant Conditioning

Operant conditioning involves associating voluntary behaviors with their consequences. As B.F. Skinner observed, "Behavior shows astonishingly similar properties" across species, from pigeons to monkeys.

### The Operant Conditioning Matrix

The impact of a consequence depends on whether something is added (Positive) or removed (Negative), and whether the goal is to increase (Reinforcement) or decrease (Punishment) a behavior.

|Action|Increase Behavior (Reinforcement)|Decrease Behavior (Punishment)|
|---|---|---|
|**Present/Add Stimulus (Positive)**|**Positive Reinforcement:** Adding a pleasant stimulus (e.g., giving a treat for studying).|**Positive Punishment:** Adding an unpleasant stimulus (e.g., a speeding ticket or snapping a rubber band on a wrist).|
|**Remove/Subtract Stimulus (Negative)**|**Negative Reinforcement:** Removing an unpleasant stimulus (e.g., a car beeping stops when the seatbelt is buckled).|**Negative Punishment:** Removing a pleasant stimulus (e.g., taking away a child’s iPad or losing social time).|

### Behavioral Shaping

**Shaping** is the process of gradually guiding behavior toward a desired goal through **successive approximations**—reinforcing actions that get closer and closer to the target behavior.

### Reinforcement Schedules and Timing

- **Immediate vs. Delayed:** Immediate reinforcement creates faster learning but may lead to quicker extinction if the reinforcer is removed.
- **Continuous vs. Partial (Intermittent):** Partial reinforcement (only reinforcing sometimes) results in more persistent learning and is harder to extinguish than continuous reinforcement.

### Punishment

Punishment is often "tricky" because:

- Behavior is suppressed rather than forgotten.
- It teaches discrimination (the behavior only stops in specific situations).
- It can teach fear of the punisher and model aggression.
- ==Punishment only tells an organism what _not_ to do, whereas reinforcement provides a clear direction for what _to_ do.==
---
## Comparing Conditioning Models

| Feature            | Classical Conditioning                                  | Operant Conditioning                                     |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Basic Idea**     | Learning associations between events we do not control. | Learning associations between behavior and consequences. |
| **Response**       | Involuntary, automatic, reflexive.                      | Voluntary, operates on environment.                      |
| **Acquisition**    | Associating events (NS paired with US).                 | Associating response with a consequence.                 |
| **Extinction**     | CR decreases when CS is presented alone.                | Responding decreases when reinforcement stops.           |
| **Cognitive Part** | Expectation that US will follow CS.                     | Expectation that a response will be reinforced/punished. |
**Caveats on conditioning, broadly**
- Behaviorists probably underestimated the role of biological constraints (remember preparedness?) 
- And the role of cognition and mental processes 
-  “Latent learning”

### Influences and Constraints on Learning

Learning is not purely a result of environmental stimuli; it is shaped by a "Bio-Psycho-Social" framework.

1. Biological Influences
- **Preparedness:** An animal’s capacity for conditioning is limited by biological constraints. For example, mammals easily associate novel tastes with nausea (a safety mechanism), while birds associate sight with safety.
- **Adaptive Responses:** Genetic predispositions and neural mirroring influence how easily certain behaviors are learned.

1. Psychological Influences
- **Cognition:** Behaviorists initially underestimated the role of mental processes.
- **Latent Learning:** Evidence shows that learning can occur without immediate reinforcement. For instance, rats in a maze who receive no food reward until day 11 show a sudden, dramatic drop in errors once a reward is introduced, proving they had been learning the maze layout all along.
- **Expectations:** The predictability of associations and individual expectations play a role in how conditioning takes hold.

1. Social-Cultural Influences
- **Modeling and Motivation:** The presence of others and culturally learned preferences affect the drive to learn and perform specific behaviors.
---
## Observational Learning

Learning frequently occurs without direct experience through **Observational Learning** (also known as social learning).

- **Modeling:** The process of observing and imitating a specific behavior.
- **Vicarious Consequences:** Individuals learn by observing others receive rewards (vicarious reinforcement) or punishments (vicarious punishment).
- **The Bobo Doll Experiment:** Research demonstrates that children who observe an adult model performing aggressive acts (hitting, sitting on, or using mallets on a doll) show significantly higher levels of imitative aggression compared to a control group.

# 24-26. Memory

## The Information-Processing Model

The memory system operates through a progression of information from external events into long-term storage. Each stage is characterized by specific durations, capacities, and functions.

**Measuring Memory**

1. **Recall:** Retrieving information not currently in conscious awareness.
2. **Recognition:** Identifying items previously learned.
3. **Relearning:** A measure of the time saved when learning material again. Ebbinghaus found that as rehearsal increases on "Day 1," the time required to relearn the information on "Day 2" decreases.

### Comparative Overview of Memory Stages

| Feature      | Sensory Memory                 | Working/Short-Term Memory (WM/STM)               | Long-Term Memory (LTM)                        |
| ------------ | ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------- |
| **Duration** | Milliseconds to a few seconds  | 20–30 seconds (unless rehearsed)                 | Relatively permanent (years)                  |
| **Capacity** | Large                          | Limited: 7 ± 2 "chunks"                          | Very large                                    |
| **Function** | Brief buffer for sensory input | Active workspace for incoming and retrieved info | Archive of knowledge, skills, and experiences |
![[Pasted image 20260320232912.png]]
### Sensory Memory

Sensory memory is the immediate, very brief recording of sensory information.

- **Sperling’s Research:** In "whole report" experiments, participants could only recall about 4.5 items. However, Sperling discovered that participants actually perceived more than they could report; the information decayed faster than it could be articulated.
- **Partial Report Results:** When using a tone cue to signal which part of an array to recall immediately after it disappeared, participants achieved 100% accuracy for 3x3 arrays and 75% for 3x4 arrays.

### Working and Short-Term Memory

Modern psychology distinguishes between a passive buffer (STM) and an active workspace (WM).

- **The "Magical Number":** Miller (1956) identified the capacity of STM as approximately **7 ± 2 items**. 
- **Chunking**: an item = a chunk. Chunking (+ mnemonics) might be nearly unlimited based on the method of loci. It allows a person to store more total information by grouping related items together. **PAO (Person–Action–Object) system**, where every two-digit number is processed as a single image (e.g., "14" becomes "Taylor Swift – singing – microphone"). By turning long sequences of numbers into single, vivid images, they can place these "chunks" into a mental "memory palace" for later retrieval.
- **Two-Track Mind:** Memory involves parallel processing. While we consciously attend to limited information (**Selective Attention**), other data is processed outside our awareness (**Unconscious Processing**).
- ![[Pasted image 20260320233528.png]]
- **Duration:** Without active rehearsal, information in this stage is transient and typically lasts less than 30 seconds.

### Long-Term Memory

LTM is a permanent and limitless archive. The "fate" of info reaching LTM is often illustrated by the **Serial Position Curve**:

- **Primacy Effect:** Better recall for items at the beginning of a list (linked to LTM).
- **Recency Effect:** Better recall for items at the end of a list (linked to WM).

### The Flow of Information

- **Encoding:** The process of moving information from sensory memory to working memory (via attention) and from working memory to long-term storage.
- **Automatic Processing:** Some information bypasses the working memory stage and is encoded directly into long-term storage (e.g., space, time, and frequency).
- **Retrieval:** The process of accessing information from the long-term store and bringing it back into the working memory workspace.

---
## Encoding: The Impact of Processing Depth

How information is initially encoded significantly dictates the success of later retrieval.

- **Levels of Processing:** Deep processing, which focuses on the meaning (semantics) of information, leads to much higher recall rates than shallow processing.
- **Intent vs. Method:** Research by Hyde & Jenkins (1973) demonstrates that the depth of processing matters more than the intention to remember. Individuals who processed words deeply without being told of a subsequent test showed similar recall levels (~0.69) to those who were told about the test, whereas shallow processors showed significantly lower recall (~0.39 to 0.43).
- **Serial Position Effect:** The fate of information is often determined by its position in a sequence.
    - **Primacy Effect:** Better recall for items at the beginning of a list (linked to long-term memory). ==Delayed test or immediate counting task: only primacy effect.==
    - **Recency Effect:** Better recall for items at the end of a list (linked to working memory).

## The Architecture of Memory Storage

Memory storage is distributed across different brain regions based on the type of information being stored.

### Explicit vs. Implicit Memory

1. Explicit Memory (Declarative)

Processed primarily in the **hippocampus** and **frontal lobes**, these memories require conscious effort to recall.

- **Semantic Memory:** Facts and general knowledge.
- **Episodic Memory:** Personally experienced events (e.g., family holidays).
- Semantic memory and episodic memory can co-occur

2. Implicit Memory (Nondeclarative)

Processed in the **cerebellum** and **basal ganglia**, these memories are formed and used without conscious recall.

- **Space, Time, and Frequency:** Automatically processed details of daily life.
- **Motor and Cognitive Skills:** Procedural tasks, such as riding a bike.
- **Classical Conditioning:** Automatic reactions, such as anxiety in a dentist’s office.
- We can use Motor and Cognitive Skills and Classical Conditioning without being able to articulate them.
- **Priming:** The unconscious activation of associations in memory.
- ![[Pasted image 20260320232515.png]]

**Biological Insights**

- **The Role of Sleep:** The **Hippocampus** acts as a temporary "loading dock" for event files before they are transferred to the cortex for permanent storage, a process called **consolidation** that occurs during sleep.
- ![[Pasted image 20260321182601.png]]
- **Emotional Memory:** The **Amygdala** boosts memory processing during stressful or emotional times. This results in **Flashbulb Memories**—vivid memories of emotionally significant events. While these feel highly accurate, they remain subject to decay and forgetting.
- **Clinical Case Studies:** Patients like **HM** and **Clive Wearing** suffered **severe anterograde amnesia** (inability to form new LTMs) due to hippocampal damage. While they could perform working memory tasks and showed intact **procedural/implicit learning** (e.g., improving at a mirror-drawing task), they had no conscious memory of having performed the tasks before.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Retrieval Mechanics

The success of retrieval is largely determined by the overlap between the conditions at the time of encoding and the conditions at the time of recall.

- **Encoding Specificity Principle:** Information is better remembered if the cues available at retrieval match the cues that were present during encoding.
- ![[Pasted image 20260321183222.png|201]]
- **Context-Dependent Memory:** External environment cues (e.g., scoring higher on a test when taken in the same environment where the material was studied).
- **State-Dependent Memory:** Internal environment cues, including mood-congruent memory. Research shows that being in the same physiological state (e.g., sober vs. intoxicated) during both study and testing improves recall compared to mismatched states.
- **Reconstruction:** We "reconstruct" the past; it is an interpretive process rather than a playback of recorded events.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## The Seven Sins of Memory

Daniel L. Schacter categorizes memory failures into seven "sins," all of which emerge from normally adaptive memory functioning.

### Sins of Omission (Forgetting)

1. **Transience:** The decreasing accessibility of information over time, caused by decay or interference.
	**Interference**:
	- **Proactive Interference (Forward-acting):** When old memories interfere with the retrieval of new information (e.g., writing 2025 as the date in 2026; The more lists of words you learn, the lower your recall).
	- **Retroactive Interference:** When new information interferes with the recall of old memories (e.g., difficulty recalling a previous address after filling out a new rental application).
2. **Absentmindedness:** A breakdown at the interface of attention and memory, often manifesting as **inattentional or change blindness**.
3. **Blocking:** Temporary inaccessibility of stored information (retrieval failure), such as the "tip-of-the-tongue" phenomenon.

### Sins of Commission (Distortion and Persistence)

1. **Misattribution:** Attributing a memory or idea to the wrong source (e.g., source memory confusion or false memories).
![[Pasted image 20260322001817.png|282]]
2. **Suggestibility:** The implantation of memories resulting from misinformation or suggestion.
	The work of Loftus and Palmer (1974) illustrates how leading questions can alter memory. In a study of car accident recall, the verb used to describe the crash significantly impacted speed estimates and the false recall of physical evidence
3. **Bias:** Retrospective distortions produced by current knowledge, beliefs, and feelings (e.g., hindsight bias; Top-down influences on memory).
### Sin of Remembering

1. **Persistence:** The intrusive or pathological remembering of unwanted events, common in post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

### Verisimilitude

**Verisimilitude** ("truth-seeming"): the appearance of fluent, verbatim episodic memories as being true, which can be highly persuasive yet inaccurate.
![[Pasted image 20260322002710.png|310]]
- Flashbulb memories 
- In court: recollections at trials are substantive evidence, recollections from the initial test are hearsay (and thus irrelevant, not considered)

## Forensic Implications: Eyewitness Memory

As of 2026, the Innocence Project reported 255 overturned convictions of innocent people. Memory failures are central to these wrongful convictions:

- 62% involved eyewitness misidentification.
- 29% involved false confessions, sometimes prompted by coercive tactics.

In legal settings, a conflict exists between "hearsay" (initial tests/recollections) and "substantive evidence" (recollections at trials). However, true memories typically weaken over time due to forgetting, while contaminated or false memories can strengthen through post-event exposure.

---
## Adaptive Features of Memory Errors

While memory errors are problematic, they highlight the adaptive nature of the system:

- **Forgetting:** Allows for quick judgments based on lingering feelings or the "gist" of a situation rather than being bogged down by irrelevant details.
- **Construction:** Enables the mind to fill in incomplete information, organize data into overarching concepts, and create useful abstractions.
- **Source Monitoring:** Often, the content of information is more vital for survival than the specific source.

---
## Strategies for Improving Memory

To improve memory, one must introduce "desirable difficulties"—training that is challenging but manageable.

### Deep Processing and Encoding

Memory is improved by **Elaborative Encoding**, which involves making connections to other topics or one's own life, rather than simple **Maintenance Repetition**.

|Encoding Type|Level of Recall|
|---|---|
|**Visual** (What the word looks like)|Lowest|
|**Acoustic** (How the word sounds)|Moderate|
|**Semantic** (What the word means/Sentence fit)|Highest|

### The Ineffectiveness of Rereading

Approximately 78% of students use rereading as their primary study strategy, yet it is considered a "low utility" technique. Rereading is time-consuming, does not result in durable memory, and creates an "unwitting self-deception" where familiarity with the text is mistaken for mastery of the content.

### High-Utility Learning Techniques

- **Practice Testing:** Actively retrieving information is far more effective than repeated study. Data shows that while "Study-Study" may be effective for 5-minute recall, "Study-Test" results in significantly higher retention after 2 days and 1 week.
- **Distributed Practice (Spacing):** Spacing study sessions over time is superior to "massed" practice (cramming).
- **Interleaved Practice:** Mixing different types of problems or materials during study.

**The "Cramming" Fallacy:** Cramming is described as "learning without remembering." Similar to physical exercise, building "strong areas of knowledge" requires practicing a bit every day rather than a single, exhausting session.

# 27. Thinking

## The Architecture of Human Thinking

Thinking involves the use and occasional misuse of information through concepts, categories, and problem-solving strategies.

### Concepts and Categories

To manage information, the mind creates "mental groupings" of similar objects, events, or people.

- **Exemplars and Prototypes:** These are mental shortcuts used to categorize new information.
- **Categorization:** While helpful for efficiency, it can be harmful when it leads to overgeneralization.

### Problem-Solving Strategies

The sources define three primary methods for reaching solutions:

1. **Algorithms:** Step-by-step procedures that guarantee a correct solution (e.g., solving a mathematical equation like c−20=4−3c).
2. **Insight:** A sudden realization of an answer without a specific strategy. This "Aha! moment" is accompanied by a burst of activity in the **right temporal lobe**.
3. **Heuristics:** "Fast and frugal" mental shortcuts that allow for quick judgments but do not guarantee accuracy.

### Top-down Processing in Thinking

Cognitive processes are often hindered by:

- **Mental Set:** The tendency to approach a problem in one specific way because it has worked in the past.
- **Fixation:** The inability to see a problem from a new perspective (e.g., the Maier string-tying problem).

Heuristics are: 
- Rules of thumb 
- Not guaranteed to give the right answer all the time

Heuristics are helpful

### The Availability Heuristic

The tendency to judge the frequency or likelihood of an event based on how easily examples come to mind.

- **Impact on Perception:** A 2020 NPR/Ipsos poll showed 47% of people believed most summer protests were violent, despite data showing 96.3% involved no property damage and 97.7% reported no injuries.
- **Personal Bias:** When asked what percentage of house cleaning they do, partners often provide totals exceeding 100% (e.g., 69% and 31%) because their own efforts are more "available" in memory.

### Confirmation Bias

The tendency to search for, interpret, and recall information that is consistent with prior beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence.

### Framing and Wording Effects

The way a choice is presented significantly influences decisions, a concept used in **Behavioral Nudging**.

- **Organ Donation:** In "Opt-In" countries like Germany, consent rates are low (~12%) and the act is seen as a "costly action." In "Opt-Out" countries like Austria, consent is nearly universal (99.98%) and seen as "ethically trivial."
- **Nudging:** Studies on 680,000 people regarding flu vaccinations show that framing a shot as "waiting for you" is more effective than simple reminders.

# Intelligence

Intelligence is defined by the APA as the ability to learn from experience, adapt to the environment, and utilize reason.

### Core Intelligence Models

Core Theories of Intelligence

Psychologists have proposed several frameworks to explain how intellectual ability is structured and utilized.

### Foundational and Cognitive Models

- **Spearman’s General Intelligence (g):** Proposes a single underlying factor that predicts abilities across various academic areas. Its strength lies in the fact that many different mental abilities correlate, though critics question if it captures the full spectrum of human capability.
- **Thurstone’s Primary Mental Abilities:** Suggests intelligence is composed of 7 distinct factors. While more informative than a single g score, these factors still tend to cluster, suggesting a single underlying g factor.
- **Cattell-Horn-Carroll (CHC) Theory:** Integrates general and specific abilities, bridged by:
    - **Fluid Intelligence:** The ability to reason speedily and abstractly (e.g., solving novel logic puzzles). This typically declines with age.
    - **Crystallized Intelligence:** Accumulated knowledge and verbal skills, which tend to remain stable or increase with age.

### Non-academic intelligences

- **Gardner’s Multiple Intelligences:** Proposes 8–9 independent intelligences (e.g., interpersonal, spatial). While it acknowledges skills beyond "school smarts," critics argue these may be "talents" rather than intelligences. Recent conceptual analyses have even labeled this theory a "neuromyth" due to a lack of empirical testability and the high correlation between supposedly independent domains.
- **Sternberg’s Triarchic Theory:** Identifies three areas that predict real-world success:
    - **Analytical:** Academic problem-solving.
    - **Creative:** Producing novel and valuable ideas (divergent thinking).
    - **Practical:** "Street smarts" and the ability to handle everyday tasks.

## The History and Measurement of Intelligence

The quest to quantify mental ability has moved from evolutionary theories to standardized clinical tools.

### Historical Milestones

- **Francis Galton:** Attempted to measure human mental potential with a focus on hereditary faculties and "evolutionary" improvement.
- **Binet & Simon:** Developed the first practical intelligence test for French schools. They introduced the concept of **Mental Age (MA)**, suggesting remedial help was needed when a child's MA was two years behind their **Chronological Age (CA)**.
- **Lewis Terman:** Adapted Binet’s work into the Stanford-Binet test and conducted a longitudinal study of "gifted" children (the "Termites"). His research debunked myths that gifted children were physically weak or "early to rot," finding they were generally well-adjusted and highly successful in adulthood.

### Modern Assessment: The Wechsler Scales (WISC/WAIS)

Current "gold standard" testing uses the Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children (WISC) and the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale (WAIS).

- **Structure:** Includes an overall IQ score and subtests for verbal comprehension, working memory, perceptual/fluid reasoning, and processing speed.
- **Standardization:** Scores are scaled to a mean (M) of 100 with a standard deviation (SD) of 15.
    - **Gifted:** IQ > 130.
    - **Intellectual Disability:** IQ ≤ 70.

| Subtest Category         | Examples of Tasks                                                     |
| ------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Verbal Comprehension** | Defining vocabulary, explaining similarities between objects.         |
| **Working Memory**       | Digit span (repeating numbers forward and backward).                  |
| **Visual-Spatial**       | Block design (recreating patterns with colored blocks).               |
| **Fluid Reasoning**      | Matrix reasoning (identifying the next figure in a logical sequence). |
| **Processing Speed**     | Symbol search, digit-symbol substitution (coding).                    |

### Stability and Trends in Intelligence Scores

1. Lifespan Stability

Intelligence measurements are remarkably stable over time. Longitudinal data (such as the Scottish Mental Surveys) show a strong correlation (r=.66) between scores at age 11 and scores at age 80. While fluid intelligence and processing speed decline in later years, crystallized intelligence (world knowledge) remains robust.

2. The Flynn Effect

Throughout the 20th century, IQ scores increased by approximately 2–3 points per decade. Potential causes include:

- Improved nutrition and education.
- Reduction of pathogen-related factors.
- Increased familiarity with visual stimuli and testing formats. Recent data suggests this trend may now be slowing, stabilizing, or reversing in some regions.

## Predictors of Real-World Success

While high IQ correlates with higher annual income and job performance (r≈.30 to .51), it is only one of several critical predictors.

### The Role of Self-Control and Grit

Research suggests that non-cognitive traits are often better predictors of life outcomes than IQ:

- **Self-Control/Self-Discipline:** The capacity to regulate emotions and behavior in the moment. The "Marshmallow Test" demonstrated that four-year-olds with higher willpower later achieved higher SAT scores, better grades, higher salaries, and lower BMIs. In some studies, self-discipline is a better predictor of GPA than IQ.
- **Grit:** Defined as a combination of **passion and perseverance**—the tendency to remain committed to long-term goals for years. Grit scores correlate with retention at West Point and success in the National Spelling Bee.

### Additional Predictors

- **Personality Traits:** Specifically **Conscientiousness** (being organized, careful, and disciplined).
- **Creativity:** The ability to produce ideas that are original, useful, and surprising.
- **Deliberate Practice:** Assiduous work toward achievement.
- **Social Skills:** Including emotional intelligence.

### Notable Exceptions

- **Savant Syndrome:** Individuals who score low on general intelligence tests but possess an "island of genius" in a specific area, such as mathematical calculation, absolute pitch, or artistic reproduction. This highlights that specific abilities can exist independently of general cognitive level.
