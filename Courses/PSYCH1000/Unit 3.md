## 1. Emotions (Modules 36-38)

### 1.1 What Do Emotions Do?

Emotions are **not** simply opponents of reason — they serve useful, adaptive purposes:

- **Functional & reactional** — they interrupt ongoing processing, focus attention, and direct cognitive resources
- **Motivational** — they motivate thought and action, providing information to guide and coordinate a person with their world
- Emotions **direct attention** toward relevant stimuli in the environment

### 1.2 Facets of Emotion

Emotions consist of five interconnected facets:

| Facet | Description |
|---|---|
| **Behavior** | Observable actions associated with the emotion |
| **Physiological changes** | Bodily responses (heart rate, sweating, etc.) |
| **Cognition** | Thoughts and appraisals |
| **Experiential** | Subjective feeling quality |
| **Action Tendencies** | Urges to act in particular ways |

### 1.3 Affective Circumplex & Temporal Nature

- The **Affective Circumplex** maps emotions along two dimensions:
	- **Valence**: Pleasant ↔ Unpleasant
	- **Arousal**: Intense (Activation) ↔ Mild (Deactivation)
- Emotional phenomena span different **time courses**:
	- *Seconds–minutes*: Expressions, autonomic changes
	- *Hours–days*: Self-reported emotions
	- *Days–weeks*: Moods
	- *Months–years*: Emotional disorders
	- *Years–lifetime*: Personality traits

### 1.4 Classic Theories of Emotion

Four seminal theorists shaped early emotion research:

| Theory | Key Idea | Formula |
|---|---|---|
| **James-Lange** | Body first → Emotion follows. We feel emotion *because* we notice bodily changes | Body → Emotion |
| **Cannon-Bard** | Feelings, cognitions, physiology & behavior happen **simultaneously** (in parallel) — not one causing the other | Body + Emotion (in parallel) |
| **Schachter-Singer** (Two-Factor) | Unexplained arousal → Examine environment → Make attribution → Emotion | Body + Interpretation → Emotion |

> *"The bodily changes follow directly the perception of the exciting fact, and our feeling of the same changes as they occur IS the emotion."* — William James, 1884

### 1.5 Current & Social Constructionist Theories

- **Social Constructionist Approach** (James Averill, 1980; Batja Mesquita):
	- Emotions are primarily **cultural products** that derive their meaning from learned social rules, not biology
	- Supporting evidence: differences in elicitors/expression, values, and language across cultures
- Current theories span multiple traditions: basic emotion, appraisal, psychological construction, and social construction (Gross & Barrett, 2011)

## 2. Stress & Health (Modules 39-40)

### 2.1 Key Terminology

| Term                 | Definition                                    |
| -------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| **Stressor**         | A challenge or threat                         |
| **Stress reaction**  | Physical and emotional response to a stressor |
| **Stress appraisal** | The process by which one interprets a threat  |

### 2.2 Types of Stressors & Set-Point Theory

Three categories of stressors:
1. **Catastrophes** — large-scale, unpredictable events
2. **Significant Life Changes** — major transitions (marriage, divorce, death)
3. **Daily Hassles** — everyday irritations and pressures

**Set-Point Theory** (Lucas, 2007):
- People tend to bounce back to a well-being "set-point" after major life events
- However, research shows that some events (e.g., unemployment, severe disability, divorce) can produce **lasting** shifts — not everyone fully returns to baseline

### 2.3 Stress Appraisal & Measurement

- A stressful event can be appraised as either:
	- **Threat** → Stressed, Distracted
	- **Challenge** → Activated, Focused
- **Trier Social Stress Task (TSST)**: A standardized lab-based stress task involving instructions, preparation, mental math, and a speech — reliably increases cortisol
	- Higher perfectionism is associated with a greater cortisol response (Wirtz et al., 2007)

### 2.4 Sleep, Stress & Health

- **Sleep disturbances precede depression and self-harm** in 8–12-year-olds (no reverse association; Hoyniak et al., 2026)
- Shorter sleep duration → **weaker antibody response** to Hepatitis B vaccination (Prather et al., 2012)
- Sleeping **< 6 hours** → significantly **greater susceptibility** to the common cold (Prather et al., 2015)

### 2.5 Well-Being Models & Strategies

**Two major models of well-being:**

| Subjective/Hedonic (Diener, 1984) | Psychological/Eudaimonic (Ryff, 1989) |
|---|---|
| Positive Affect | Sense of Purpose |
| (Low) Negative Affect | Personal Growth |
| Life Satisfaction | Self-Acceptance |
| | Autonomy |
| | Environmental Mastery |
| | Positive Relationships |

**Benefits of well-being**: predicts positive life events, associated with better physical/cognitive health and prosocial behavior (Salovey, 1990)

**Nine evidence-based strategies** to increase well-being:
1. Physical activity
2. Adequate sleep
3. Nutritious diet
4. Relationships
5. Helping others
6. Meaningful activities
7. Pleasurable activities
8. Mindfulness meditation
9. Humor & laughter

### 2.6 Emotion Regulation & Coping

**Emotion Regulation** = The shaping of which emotions one has, when one has them, and how one experiences or expresses them.

**Gross's Process Model of ER** — Five families ordered by timing:

| Strategy | Description |
|---|---|
| **Situation Selection** | Leaving or entering situations to experience/avoid emotions |
| **Situation Modification** | Modifying external aspects to alter emotional impact |
| **Attentional Deployment** | Directing attention within a situation to influence emotions |
| **Reappraisal** | Changing how one appraises a situation to change its emotional impact |
| **Response Modulation** | Directly influencing experiential, behavioral, or physiological components |

**Key insight**: No strategy is universally good or bad. Using strategies **flexibly** is most adaptive (Bonanno & Burton, 2013).

**Coping strategies**: (1) Emotion regulation, (2) Social support, (3) Physical activity, (4) Relaxation (e.g., Progressive Muscle Relaxation)

## 3. Introduction to Psychological Disorders (Module 48)

### 3.1 Critical Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Symptoms** | Thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that indicate a mental disorder |
| **Syndrome/Disorder** | A group of symptoms that appear together and represent a specific type of disorder |

**Important features** of disorders:
- **Duration & Frequency** — symptoms must persist over time
- **Functional Impairment** — interferes with daily life
- **Personal Distress** — causes suffering to the individual

### 3.2 Classification Systems & the DSM

**Why classify?**

| Benefits                                     | Limitations                             |
| -------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| Facilitates description & communication      | May lead to stigmatization              |
| Aids treatment decisions & prognosis         | Unrelated problems may be misattributed |
| Facilitates research on etiology & treatment | Expectations for behavior may change    |
| Facilitates 3rd-party reimbursement          |                                         |

**DSM (Diagnostic & Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders)**:
- Published by the American Psychiatric Association
- Current edition: **DSM-5-TR** (2022)
- History: DSM-I (1952) → DSM-II (1968) → DSM-III (1980) → DSM-IV (1994) → DSM-5 (2013) → DSM-5-TR (2022)
- Disorders identified by **symptoms** (not etiology/cause)
- Relies on observations and descriptions rather than ideas about causes

### 3.3 Categorical vs. Dimensional Approaches

| Categorical Approach | Dimensional Approach |
|---|---|
| "Yes" or "No" — qualitative difference | "How much?" — quantitative difference |
| People are in discrete groups | People exist on a continuum |
| Largely used by DSM-5 | Continuum from "not at all" to "very" |

## 4. Anxiety Disorders & OCD (Module 49)

### 4.1 Defining Anxiety

Anxiety involves three interconnected components:
- **Thoughts**: "What if I fail?" / "They don't really like me"
- **Feelings**: Anxious, worried, fearful
- **Behaviors**: Avoidance, physiological changes

**Yerkes-Dodson Law**: Moderate arousal optimizes performance; too little causes fatigue, too much causes impaired performance.

### 4.2 Types of Anxiety Disorders

All anxiety disorders share: symptoms are **provoking**, **persistent**, **excessive/unreasonable**, **impairing**, and patients **avoid or suffer through** them.

What **differs** is what the person fears:

| Disorder                               | Core Fear                                   |
| -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| **Specific Phobia**                    | Fear of a specific object/situation         |
| **Social Anxiety Disorder**            | Concern about social evaluation             |
| **Panic Disorder**                     | Recurrent panic attacks                     |
| **Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)** | Chronic, pervasive worry                    |
| **Agoraphobia**                        | Fear of anxiety itself / fear of situations |
| **OCD**                                | Obsessions and/or compulsions               |

### 4.3 Epidemiology & Comorbidity

- Anxiety disorders are **more common than any other mental disorder** (~18% 1-year prevalence in the US)
- Highest lifetime prevalence: Specific Phobia (~12%), followed by SAD, GAD
- High **comorbidity**: multiple anxiety disorders often co-occur, and anxiety frequently co-occurs with Major Depressive Disorder

### 4.4 OCD: Obsessions & Compulsions

**Obsessions**:
1. Recurrent, persistent thoughts/urges/images that are intrusive, unwanted, and cause anxiety
2. Individual attempts to ignore, suppress, or neutralize them

**Common obsession types**: Contamination, Repeated doubts, Ordering/arranging, Violent/aggressive thoughts, Sexual images

**Compulsions**:
1. Repetitive behaviors or mental acts performed in response to obsessions or rigid rules
2. Designed to prevent/reduce anxiety or prevent a dreaded event — but are not realistically connected or are clearly excessive

**Common compulsion types**: Checking, Decontamination (washing/cleaning), Repeating, Ordering/Arranging

> Everyone has unwanted intrusive thoughts — what distinguishes OCD is the **degree of distress and functional impairment**.

**Thought Suppression**: Trying *not* to think about something (e.g., a white bear) paradoxically increases the thought — relevant to understanding OCD maintenance.

### 4.5 OCD Treatment

**Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP)**:
- Information gathering about symptoms to monitor behaviors
- Repeated, prolonged exposure to anxiety-provoking situations
- Instructions to **refrain** from performing ritual behaviors

## 5. Mood Disorders (Module 50)

### 5.1 Types of Mood Disorders

| Unipolar Disorders | Bipolar Disorders |
|---|---|
| Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) | Bipolar I Disorder |
| Persistent Depressive Disorder | Bipolar II Disorder |
| | Cyclothymic Disorder |

Mood disorders are **episodic** — they consist of distinct episodes (major depressive episodes, manic episodes).

### 5.2 Major Depressive Episode (MDE)

An MDE is **more than just feeling depressed**. The 9 symptoms (need 5+ for ≥2 weeks):

1. **Depressed mood** *(required or #2)*
2. **Anhedonia** — loss of interest or pleasure *(required or #1)*
3. Weight OR appetite change
4. Sleep disturbance
5. Psychomotor agitation OR retardation
6. Fatigue
7. Worthlessness OR excessive guilt
8. Inability to concentrate OR indecisiveness
9. Thoughts of death, suicidal ideation, plan or attempt

> Symptoms often **vary by age** — children, preadolescents, and older adults may present differently.

### 5.3 Manic Episode

Symptoms of a manic episode (need 3+ alongside elevated/irritable mood, lasting ≥1 week):

1. **Elevated, expansive, or irritable mood** *(required)*
2. Inflated self-esteem or grandiosity
3. Decreased need for sleep
4. More talkative / pressure to keep talking
5. Flight of ideas or racing thoughts
6. Distractibility
7. Increase in goal-directed activity or psychomotor agitation
8. Excessive involvement in pleasurable activities with painful consequences

Must cause **impairment, hospitalization, or psychotic features** and cannot be due to substances or a medical condition.

### 5.4 MDD vs. Bipolar I

| | Major Depressive Disorder | Bipolar I Disorder |
|---|---|---|
| **Manic Episode** | None | At least one required |
| **Major Depressive Episode** | Required | Not required, but common |

### 5.5 Epidemiology

- **Gender**: Women experience MDE and mood disorders at roughly **2x the rate** of men; manic episodes show smaller gender differences
- **Race/Ethnicity**: Lifetime prevalence of MDE is highest among White individuals; manic episode differences across race are **not statistically significant**
- **Age**: Depression is more prevalent in **recent generations** (cohort effect); occurs frequently in the elderly
- Depression is **increasingly prevalent** in younger cohorts (ages 18-29 show highest cumulative lifetime prevalence)

### 5.6 Treatments for Depression

Evidence-based treatments include:
- Cognitive Therapy
- Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)
- Problem-Solving Therapy
- Interpersonal Psychotherapy (IPT)
- Motivational Interviewing
- Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT)

## 6. Schizophrenia (Module 51)

### 6.1 Positive Symptoms

"Positive" = an **excess or distortion** of normal functions (not "good"):

**Hallucinations**:
- Sensory experiences that seem real but occur without external stimuli
- Can involve any of the five senses
- Often have personal relevance for the patient

**Delusions**:
- Rigidly held inaccurate beliefs or misrepresentations of reality
- May be connected to hallucinations
- Assessed by: degree of conviction, preoccupation, not shared by others, personal reference, difficulty resisting, behavior change

### 6.2 Negative Symptoms

"Negative" = aspects of normal behavior that **should be present but are absent**:

| Category | Symptoms |
|---|---|
| **Diminished Emotional Expression** | Blunted affect, Alogia (reduced speech) |
| **Diminished Motivation & Pleasure** | Avolition (lack of motivation), Asociality, Anhedonia |

### 6.3 Disorganization

Reflects bizarre behaviors and disturbances in thinking:

- **Disorganized Speech** — difficult to follow, conveys little meaning, slips between topics
- **Catatonic Behavior** — decreased awareness/reactivity, immobility, muscular rigidity, purposeless/repetitious motor activity
- **Grossly Disorganized Behavior** — ranges from childlike silliness to unpredictable agitation

### 6.4 Phases & Epidemiology

**Three phases**: Prodromal → Active → Residual

**Epidemiology**:
- Lifetime prevalence of psychotic disorder ≈ 4%; ~1% have schizophrenia
- **Sex differences**: Men tend to have higher rates, earlier onset, worse premorbid functioning, and a more severe course
- Age of onset: Men peak at 15–24; women peak later at 25–34 (Haffner et al., 1998)

### 6.5 Medications & Course

| Generation                            | Era   | Mechanism                                  | Adverse Effects                                 |
| ------------------------------------- | ----- | ------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------- |
| **1st Gen / Typical Antipsychotics**  | 1950s | Block dopamine receptors                   | Extrapyramidal symptoms, **Tardive Dyskinesia** |
| **2nd Gen / Atypical Antipsychotics** | 1990s | Block dopamine receptors (cortical/limbic) | Weight gain/obesity, some TD risk               |

- **Clozapine** is considered by some to be the gold standard
- **Relapse rates**: 65–70% in first year without medication; ~40% with antipsychotic drugs
- Medication compliance is a major challenge

## 7. Psychological Treatments (Modules 54-55)

### 7.1 Three Waves of CBT

| Wave    | Name                       | Focus                                          | Core Idea                                                                 |
| ------- | -------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **1st** | Behavior Therapy           | Change **behavior**                            | Based on learning theory; learn new, adaptive responses                   |
| **2nd** | Cognitive Behavior Therapy | Change **thoughts**                            | Distress caused by distorted thinking; identify/modify unhelpful thoughts |
| **3rd** | Third-Wave CBT             | Change **relationship** to thoughts & emotions | Mindfulness, acceptance, flexibility; notice without becoming stuck       |

**Common components** of CBT:
- Present-focused, short-term, progress-monitored
- Establishing rapport, psychoeducation, goal setting, collecting info, teaching skills, collaborative empiricism

**CBT Procedures**:
- Monitor maladaptive self-statements (antecedents & consequences)
- Evaluate evidence, substitute rational cognitions
- Rehearsal (e.g., daily thought logs) — similar to a science experiment

> Psychotherapy is associated with **changes in brain function**.

### 7.2 Evaluating Treatments

- **Manualized treatments**: Therapy protocols written in manual format to standardize delivery
- **Evidence-based treatments**: Specific treatments for specific populations/disorders with research support
- **Evidence-based practice**: Using research-supported treatments + clinical expertise + patient characteristics/preferences

### 7.3 Psychotherapy Outcome Research

Meta-analyses show:
- Therapy patients are **better off than untreated peers**
- Psychotherapy has **longer-lasting benefits than medications**
- Results are **comparable to or better than** many physical health treatments

**Caveats**:
- Improvement often happens early in therapy
- Not everyone responds to a given treatment
- Research samples may not represent real-world patients
- Therapist factors and the therapeutic relationship matter significantly

## 8. Personality (Modules 45-47)

### 8.1 Psychoanalytic Approach (Freud & Jung)

**Sigmund Freud** — Father of psychoanalysis:
- Personality as a product of typical or atypical development
- Development occurs in stages, with heavy emphasis on early life
- Focused on the **unconscious** and its processes

**The Unconscious** — features of the psyche outside conscious awareness:

| Structure | Role |
|---|---|
| **Id** | Urges, desires, basic instincts |
| **Ego** | Sense of self; rational; mediates between Id and Superego |
| **Superego** | Morality; develops in middle childhood; counterbalances the Id |

**Psychosexual Development Stages**: Oral (0–1) → Anal (1–3) → Phallic (3–6) → Latency (6–puberty) → Genital (puberty+)

**Defense Mechanisms**:
- **Denial** — refusing to believe painful realities
- **Projection** — attributing one's own attributes to another
- **Rationalization** — finding justifying explanations
- **Displacement** — shifting impulses to another target
- **Reaction Formation** — switching impulses to their opposite
- **Regression** — retreating to an earlier developmental stage

**Carl Jung** — Collective Unconscious:
- Universal unconscious united by common phenomenological truths
- **Archetypes**: universal symbols of the human condition
- Founder of personality typology (Extraversion/Introversion)

### 8.2 Humanistic Approach (Maslow, Rogers, Horney, Erikson)

**Abraham Maslow**: Hierarchy of Needs → **Self-Actualization** (achieving full psychological potential; constant and life-long process)

**Carl Rogers** — Father of Humanism:
- Personality = relationship between **real self** and **ideal self**
- **Incongruence**: The gap between real and ideal self
- **Self-Actualization**: When real and ideal self are congruent

**Humanist Treatment Principles**:
- **Genuineness** — being authentic and transparent
- **Unconditional Positive Regard** — acceptance by default
- **Accurate Empathy** — treating the client's world as their own

**Karen Horney** — Psychoanalytic Social Theory:
- Founder of "Feminist Psychology"
- Focused on social pressures as drivers of neuroses
- The "ideal self" can be a product of unrealistic social pressures

**Erik Erikson** — Identity Crisis
Psychosocial Development (8 life-long stages):

| Conflict | Stage | Virtue |
|---|---|---|
| Trust vs. Mistrust | Infancy | Hope |
| Autonomy vs. Shame | Toddler | Will |
| Initiative vs. Guilt | Preschool | Purpose |
| Industry vs. Inferiority | Middle Childhood | Competence |
| Identity vs. Role Confusion | Adolescence | Fidelity |
| Intimacy vs. Isolation | Early Adulthood | Love |
| Generativity vs. Stagnation | Middle Adulthood | Care |
| Integrity vs. Despair | Late Adulthood | Wisdom |

### 8.3 Modern Trait Theories (Allport, Eysenck, Cattell, Big Five)

**Personality Trait** = A broad, enduring characteristic describing anyone in terms of thoughts, feelings, and behaviors, on a spectrum.

Inter-Individual Differences & Intra-Individual Difference

**Gordon Allport**:
- **Lexical Approach**: 4,500 adjectives organized into categories
- **Idiographic vs. Nomothetic**: Individual-specific vs. universal frameworks
- **3-level hierarchy**: Cardinal Traits (rare, defining) → Central Traits (handful everyone has) → Secondary Traits (situation-specific)

**Hans Eysenck** — **PEN Model**: Psychoticism, Extraversion, Neuroticism
- Traits are fundamentally biological and largely unchanging
- Hierarchical structure: Specific Response → Habitual Response → Traits → Super-Factors

**Raymond Cattell** — **16 Personality Factors**:
- Pioneered empirical (vs. theoretical) methods for estimating traits
- Distinguished **Source Traits** from **Surface Traits**

**Big Five** (Goldberg & John) — **OCEAN**:

| Trait | Description |
|---|---|
| **O**penness | Open to new experiences/ideas; creative |
| **C**onscientiousness | Hard-working, organized |
| **E**xtraversion | Enjoys people and conversations |
| **A**greeableness | Compassionate, polite, empathic |
| **N**euroticism | Proclivity for negative emotion; worry, depression |

**McCrae & Costa — Five Factor Theory (FFT)**:
- Big Five are fundamental, biologically-based traits
- **True Traits**: biologically determined, do not change
- **Characteristic Adaptations**: small behavioral/thought/feeling adjustments

### 8.4 Trait Change & the Maturity Principle

**Types of Change**:
- **Mean-Level Change**: Universal, systematic shifts on traits across the lifespan
- **Rank-Order Change**: Person-specific change relative to peers

**Maturity Principle** (Brent Roberts):
- People generally become **more Conscientious, more Agreeable, and less Neurotic** with age
- Change occurs across the **whole lifespan**, not just early life

**Child Temperament**: In young children, the Big 5 don't emerge; instead, 3 factors:
1. Positive Emotionality / Surgency
2. Negative Emotionality
3. Effortful Control

**Whole Trait Theory** (Fleeson):
- People have underlying **traits** (broad tendencies over time) AND **states** (moment-to-moment fluctuations)
- More differences **within-people** than between-people in momentary personality

### 8.5 Measurement & Limitations

- Personality measured via **Likert scales** (e.g., "I like to party" rated 1–5)
- **MBTI** (Myers-Briggs): Inspired by Jung's types; popular in business but has serious limitations — fails to capture the spectrum of individual differences
- Key takeaway: Traits are universal, spectral, and change across the lifespan; people vary uniquely moment-to-moment

## 9. Personality Disorders (Module 52.2)

### 9.1 What Are Personality Disorders?

- **Enduring patterns** of perceiving, relating to, and thinking about oneself and the environment that cause **impairment and/or distress**
- Evident by adolescence and **stable** across the adult lifespan
- **Ego-syntonic** (consistent with self-image; don't bother the person) — vs. other disorders which are **ego-dystonic** (dissonant with self-image)

Why study PDs?
- Significant functional impairment
- Risk factor for other mental disorders
- Important treatment implications

### 9.2 PD Clusters Overview

| Cluster A: Odd & Eccentric | Cluster B: Dramatic, Emotional, Erratic | Cluster C: Anxious, Fearful, Avoidant |
| -------------------------- | --------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Paranoid PD                | **Narcissistic PD**                     | Avoidant PD                           |
| Schizoid PD                | **Antisocial PD**                       | Dependent PD                          |
| Schizotypal PD             | **Borderline PD**                       | Obsessive-Compulsive PD               |
|                            | Histrionic PD                           |                                       |

### 9.3 Narcissistic PD

**Definition**: Pervasive pattern of grandiosity, need for admiration, and lack of empathy (5+ of 9 criteria):
1. Grandiose sense of self-importance
2. Fantasies of unlimited success/power/brilliance
3. Believes they are "special" and unique
4. Requires excessive admiration
5. Sense of entitlement
6. Interpersonally exploitive
7. Lacks empathy
8. Envious of others / believes others are envious
9. Arrogant behaviors/attitudes

**Associated features**: Unrealistically high self-view, overly sensitive to criticism, ambitious, relationship problems. Treatment is possible but slow, long-term, and requires a strong therapeutic alliance.

### 9.4 Antisocial PD & Psychopathy

**Antisocial PD**: Pervasive disregard for and violation of others' rights (3+ of 7):
1. Repeatedly breaks the law
2. Deceitfulness
3. Impulsivity / failure to plan ahead
4. Irritability or aggressiveness
5. Reckless disregard for safety
6. Consistent irresponsibility
7. Lack of remorse

**Psychopathy** (related but distinct):
- **Not** a DSM-5 diagnostic category
- Captures a **subset** of those with ASPD
- Includes emotional/interpersonal features (glib, shallow emotions, lack of guilt) and social deviance (impulsive, poor behavior controls)
- Better predictor of **recidivism** than ASPD alone

> ASPD ≠ Criminality — many people with ASPD are not criminals, and many criminals do not have ASPD.

### 9.5 Borderline PD & DBT

**Borderline PD**: Pervasive pattern of instability in relationships, self-image, and affects + marked impulsivity (5+ of 9):
1. Frantic efforts to avoid abandonment
2. Unstable, intense relationships
3. Identity disturbance
4. Self-damaging impulsivity
5. Recurrent suicidal behavior/gestures/threats
6. Affective instability
7. Chronic feelings of emptiness
8. Inappropriate, intense anger
9. Transient, stress-related paranoia or severe dissociation

**Etiology**: Childhood maltreatment is a significant risk factor.

**Treatment — Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)**:
- Reconceptualizes BPD as **dysregulation** of self, relationships, and emotions
- Main goal: address emotional dysregulation
- Four skill modules: **Mindfulness**, **Distress Tolerance**, **Emotion Regulation**, **Interpersonal Effectiveness**

## 10. Social Psychology I — Thinking & Influence (Modules 41-42)

### 10.1 Attribution Theory

**Attribution** = how we explain people's behavior.

| Type | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Dispositional Attribution** | Judging behavior based on the person's character/traits | "They're late because they're unreliable" |
| **Situational Attribution** | Judging behavior based on circumstances | "They're late because of unexpected traffic" |

**Key Biases**:

| Bias | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Fundamental Attribution Error (FAE)** | Tendency to **overemphasize traits** and underestimate situational factors when explaining **others'** behavior |
| **Actor-Observer Bias** | Actors attribute their behavior to the **situation**; observers attribute it to the actor's **disposition** |
| **Self-Serving Bias** | Taking personal credit for **success** but blaming external causes for **failure** |

> Attributions matter because how we explain events impacts our **attitudes, emotions, and behavior** (e.g., attributions about poverty, a roommate not doing dishes).

### 10.2 Cognitive Dissonance

**Cognitive Dissonance** = The uncomfortable tension when attitudes, beliefs, or behaviors **do not align**.

**Examples**:
- Buying an item you can't afford
- Eating junk food while trying to be healthy
- Cheating on an exam while thinking of yourself as honest

**Cognitive Dissonance Theory** (Festinger): People act to **reduce discomfort** by:
1. **Changing attitudes** to match behavior
2. **Adding justifications** to rationalize behavior

### 10.3 Conformity & Obedience

**Conformity** = Adjusting behavior or thinking to coincide with a group standard.
- Driven by **normative social influence** (desire to avoid rejection, gain approval)

**When are people more likely to conform?**
- Made to feel incompetent
- With three or more people
- Admire the group's status
- Have not made a prior response

**Obedience — Milgram's Experiment**:
- Participants administered (fake) electric shocks to a "learner" under an experimenter's orders
- **Majority continued to the highest shock levels**

**When is obedience highest?**
- Prestigious institution backed the authority figure
- Victim was in another room
- Person giving orders was nearby and perceived as legitimate
- No examples of defiance from others

> **Take-home**: Strong social influence can induce people to conform to falsehoods or capitulate to cruelty. **Cruelty does not require villains.**

## 11. Social Psychology II — Antisocial Relations (Module 43)

### 11.1 Prejudice, Stereotypes & Discrimination

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Prejudice** | Unjustified and negative **attitude** toward a group |
| **Stereotypes** | Generalized **beliefs** about a group of people |
| **Discrimination** | Acting in harmful/unjustifiable ways toward group members (**behavior**) |

### 11.2 Targets of Prejudice

Prejudice targets: Race/ethnicity, Gender, Sexual orientation, Physical ability, Mental health, Religion

**Racial & Ethnic Prejudice**:
- Black men judged more harshly for stereotypically associated crimes
- Health professionals allocate more resources to White vs. Black patients
- Black men are 2.5x more likely than White men to be killed by police in the US

**Gender Prejudice**:
- Gender pay gap persists for same credentials/positions
- Dads perceived as smarter than moms; sons valued over daughters
- Masculine norms are disproportionately valued and rewarded

**Sexual Orientation Prejudice**:
- LGBTQ+ people in most countries cannot openly disclose identity
- Same-sex marriage bans correlated with increased mental health disorders in gay/lesbian people — but not in straight people or in other states

### 11.3 Causes of Prejudice

Three root causes:

1. **Social Inequalities & Division**:
	- **Just-World Belief** — tendency to believe the world is fair (victims deserve their fate)
	- **Ingroup Bias** — tendency to favor one's own group

2. **Negative Emotions**:
	- **Scapegoat Theory** — prejudice provides an outlet for anger by providing someone to blame

3. **Cognitive Shortcuts**:
	- **Outgroup Homogeneity** — perceiving members of other groups as more similar to each other than they actually are

> These processes **do not happen in a vacuum** — they occur in the context of history, policy, society, and culture.

### 11.4 Reducing Implicit Bias

Many institutions now use **implicit bias trainings**. Three strategies for individuals:
1. **Mindset** — acknowledging that bias exists and committing to change
2. **Debiasing** — actively working to counter stereotypic associations
3. **Decoupling** — separating automatic associations from behavioral responses

## 12. Social Psychology III — Prosocial Relations (Module 44)

### 12.1 Attraction: Proximity & Modern Matchmaking

**Attraction** = The action or power of evoking interest, pleasure, or liking for someone or something.

Three major factors in attraction:
- **Proximity**, **Modern Matchmaking**, **Physical Attractiveness**

**Proximity**: Physical closeness increases the likelihood of attraction — simply being near someone raises familiarity and liking.

**Modern Matchmaking (Dating Apps)**:

*Who uses dating apps?*

| Group | Trend |
|---|---|
| Age | Younger adults (18–29) are the largest user group |
| Location | Urban > Rural |
| Gender | Men > Women |
| Sexual orientation | LGBTQ+ > Straight |
| Education | College degree > HS diploma |

*Key findings on online dating psychology*:
- **Fearing rejection can elicit rejection** — anxiety about being turned down becomes self-fulfilling
- **More options = more superficial decisions** — paradox of choice leads to shallower evaluation
- **Similarity is in the eye of the beholder** — people perceive more similarity with partners they are already attracted to

### 12.2 Attraction: Physical Attractiveness

Physical attractiveness:
- **Most impacts first impressions** — it is the strongest influence in initial encounters
- **Predicts how much people date and how popular they feel**
- **Is unrelated to self-esteem and happiness** — attractive people do not report higher well-being

### 12.3 Altruism & the Bystander Effect

**Altruism** = Unselfish regard for the welfare of others.

> *"Are people naturally helpful toward others and society corrupts them, or are they naturally selfish toward others and society teaches them better?"* — Warneken & Tomasello (2009)

**Bystander Effect**: The tendency for any given bystander to be **less likely to give aid if others are present**.

Two mechanisms explain this:

| Mechanism                       | Definition                                                                      |
| ------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Diffusion of Responsibility** | A person takes less responsibility for acting because others are also present   |
| **Pluralistic Ignorance**       | Looking to others' reactions to decide whether something is really an emergency |

**Decision-Making Process for Bystander Intervention** (Darley & Latané, 1968):

```
Notices incident? → Interprets as emergency? → Assumes responsibility? → Attempts to help
       ↓ No               ↓ No                      ↓ No
    No help             No help                   No help
```

All three gates must open for helping behavior to occur.

### 12.4 Factors That Increase Helping

Odds of helping **increase** when the helper:
- Is **not in a hurry**
- **Feels guilt**
- Is in a **good mood**
- Has **observed someone else be helpful**
- Is **focused on others** (not self-absorbed)
- Is in a **small town or rural area**

And when the person needing help:
- **Appears to need and deserve help**
- Is **similar to the helper**
- Is **a woman**

### 12.5 Social Norms, Contact & Cooperation

**Social Norms Governing Helping**:

| Norm                           | Definition                                                   |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Reciprocity Norm**           | Expectation that people will help those who have helped them |
| **Social Responsibility Norm** | Expectation that people will help those who need it          |

**Contact**: Positive contact between groups **typically helps** reduce hostility and increase prosocial behavior.

**Cooperation**: **Superordinate goals** (shared goals that require collaboration across group lines) override differences and bring people together.
