# Dismantling Permanent Punishment: A White Paper on Criminal Record Reform, Fair Chance Employment, and Reentry Policy for the State of Michigan

**Prepared for:** Governor Gretchen Whitmer and the Michigan State Legislature

**Subject:** Comprehensive reentry reform through automatic record sealing, ban-the-box employment protections, fair chance housing, and voting rights restoration

---

## Executive Summary

Every year, more than 600,000 people are released from state and federal prisons in the United States and return to communities that are structurally designed to exclude them (Travis, Western, & Redburn, 2014). They face a sprawling web of legal barriers — to employment, housing, public benefits, professional licenses, and the ballot box — that operate as a form of permanent, invisible punishment extending far beyond the sentence a judge ever imposed. In Michigan alone, tens of thousands of residents carry criminal records that follow them for life, even when they have not reoffended in decades. Sociological research demonstrates that these collateral consequences fall disproportionately on Black Americans, compounding racial inequality in ways that are measurable, durable, and largely invisible to policymakers. This white paper identifies the problem of permanent criminal-record punishment, reviews the sociological evidence on its consequences, engages the key controversies surrounding reform, and proposes a four-part policy package: automatic record sealing, ban-the-box employment legislation, fair chance housing protections, and automatic restoration of voting rights upon release from incarceration.

---

## I. The Problem: Criminal Records as Permanent Punishment

The United States incarcerates more people than any nation on earth — approximately 1.9 million on any given day, with an additional 3.6 million on probation and over 800,000 on parole (Prison Policy Initiative, 2022). But the formal sentence — the years behind bars — captures only a fraction of the punishment the system delivers. When a person is released, they carry a criminal record that, in most states, is permanent, public, and disqualifying across virtually every domain of civic and economic life.

Kirk and Wakefield (2017) catalogued the extraordinary breadth of what scholars call *collateral consequences*: legal restrictions that attach automatically to criminal convictions, often without judicial consideration or even the defendant's awareness. Across the fifty states, there are more than 40,000 such restrictions. They include bars on employment in licensed occupations (healthcare, education, transportation, cosmetology, plumbing), exclusion from public housing, ineligibility for federal student loans and food assistance, and — in many states — the loss of the right to vote. These consequences are not part of the sentence pronounced in court. They are administrative, regulatory, and statutory penalties that accumulate silently and persist indefinitely.

The scale of the affected population is staggering. An estimated 70 to 100 million Americans — nearly one in three adults — have some form of criminal record (National Employment Law Project, 2021). Michigan is no exception: the state's prison population has grown dramatically over the past four decades, and the vast majority of people who have served time eventually return to communities where they must navigate this web of exclusion. Garland (2001) argues that mass imprisonment has become a defining social institution, one whose consequences extend far beyond the prison walls to reshape entire communities and social structures. The criminal record is the mechanism through which that reshaping occurs — it is the lasting mark of the carceral state, carried by individuals long after they have served their time.

## II. The Evidence: How Criminal Records Perpetuate Racial and Economic Inequality

The most powerful evidence of how criminal records function as instruments of exclusion comes from Devah Pager's (2003) landmark audit study, "The Mark of a Criminal Record." Pager sent matched pairs of young men — identical in résumé, presentation, and demeanor, differing only in race and the presence of a criminal record — to apply for real entry-level jobs in Milwaukee. The findings were striking. White applicants with a criminal record received callbacks 17% of the time, compared to 34% for white applicants without a record — a 50% reduction. For Black applicants, a criminal record reduced callbacks from 14% to 5%. But the most revealing finding was the comparison across race: Black applicants *without* any criminal record (14%) received fewer callbacks than white applicants *with* a felony drug conviction (17%). The criminal record penalty was severe, but it compounded with racial stigma to produce a double disadvantage that rendered Black men with records virtually unemployable in the formal labor market.

This finding is not merely academic. Western and Pettit (2010) demonstrate that mass incarceration has created what they call an "invisible inequality" — a form of social stratification that is systematically excluded from standard economic statistics. Because incarcerated and formerly incarcerated people are missing from household surveys, unemployment figures, and wage data, the official portrait of American racial progress is misleadingly optimistic. When the incarcerated population is included, the Black-white gap in employment and earnings is significantly larger than conventional measures suggest. Western, Kling, and Weiman (2002) further show that incarceration itself depresses future earnings by 10–20%, independent of the criminal record's effect on hiring — meaning that the economic penalty is both immediate (hiring discrimination) and cumulative (wage depression over a lifetime).

The ethnographic literature brings these statistics to life. Bruce Western's *Homeward* (2018) follows men and women leaving prison in the Boston area, documenting the daily reality of reentry: the fruitless job searches, the housing applications rejected on background check, the struggle to rebuild family relationships while under the surveillance of parole. Reuben Miller's *Halfway Home* (2021) paints an even more vivid portrait of what he calls "the afterlife of mass incarceration" — the way the carceral system extends its reach into every aspect of a formerly incarcerated person's existence, from the halfway house to the parole office to the employer who will never call back. Miller argues that reentry is not a transition back to freedom; it is a continuation of punishment in a different form.

The racial dimension of this system cannot be overstated. Michelle Alexander (2012) argues that mass incarceration functions as a "new Jim Crow" — a system of racial control that, while formally race-neutral, operates through criminal law to relegate Black Americans to a permanent second-class status. The criminal record is the badge of that status. Khalil Gibran Muhammad (2010) traces the historical roots of this association, showing how Black criminality was *constructed* as a statistical and cultural category in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, providing the intellectual framework that would later justify racialized policing and sentencing. Forman (2012) complicates this narrative by noting that Black communities themselves have sometimes supported tough-on-crime policies, but he agrees that the outcomes are racially devastating regardless of intent.

## III. Key Controversies

Reform of criminal-record policy is not without controversy, and a credible white paper must engage the strongest objections.

**Public safety.** The most common objection to record sealing is that employers and landlords need access to criminal history information to protect the public. This concern is legitimate but overstated. Criminological research consistently shows that the risk of reoffending declines sharply with time: after approximately seven years without a new offense, a person with a prior record poses no greater statistical risk than a person drawn randomly from the general population (Blumstein & Nakamura, 2009). Automatic sealing after a defined crime-free period does not conceal active risk; it removes a stigma that has outlived any predictive value. Moreover, current policy is not calibrated to risk — it imposes blanket, lifetime exclusions regardless of the offense, time elapsed, or evidence of rehabilitation.

**Employer rights and liability.** Employers argue that ban-the-box laws restrict their ability to make informed hiring decisions and expose them to negligent-hiring liability. However, ban-the-box legislation does not prohibit employers from ever considering criminal history — it delays the inquiry until after a conditional offer, allowing applicants to be evaluated on their qualifications first. This sequencing reduces the risk that criminal history serves as a proxy for race (as Pager's study demonstrates it does) while preserving the employer's ability to make individualized assessments. Several states with ban-the-box laws have also enacted safe-harbor provisions that shield employers from negligent-hiring claims when they follow the prescribed process.

**The abolitionist critique.** On the other end of the spectrum, scholars like Angela Davis (2003) and Mariame Kaba (2021) argue that reforms focused on easing reentry are insufficient because they leave the fundamental structure of mass incarceration intact. From this perspective, record sealing and ban-the-box laws treat symptoms while ignoring the disease — the carceral system itself. This is a serious intellectual challenge. This paper's position is pragmatic: while structural transformation may be the long-term goal, the 70 million Americans currently carrying criminal records cannot wait for systemic abolition. Incremental reforms that demonstrably improve material conditions — employment, housing, civic participation — are both morally urgent and politically achievable. As Gottschalk (2015) argues in *Caught*, the scale of the carceral state demands a multi-front strategy that includes both immediate harm reduction and longer-term structural change.

## IV. Proposed Policy Reforms

This paper recommends that Michigan adopt a four-part reentry reform package:

**1. Automatic record sealing.** Michigan should enact legislation providing for the automatic sealing of criminal records after a defined crime-free period — seven years for felonies and three years for misdemeanors — without requiring a petition, court hearing, or attorney. The current petition-based expungement system, while recently expanded, reaches only a tiny fraction of eligible individuals because it requires legal knowledge, filing fees, and court appearances that most formerly incarcerated people cannot access (Prescott & Starr, 2020). Automatic sealing shifts the burden from the individual to the state, ensuring that the benefit reaches the people who need it most. Sealed records would remain accessible to law enforcement for future criminal investigations but would be removed from commercial background-check databases, restoring practical access to employment and housing.

**2. Ban-the-box legislation for public and private employers.** Michigan should prohibit all employers — public and private — from inquiring about criminal history on initial job applications. Criminal history disclosure should be delayed until after a conditional offer of employment has been made, at which point the employer may conduct an individualized assessment considering the nature of the offense, time elapsed, and relevance to the position. This sequencing is critical: Pager's (2003) research shows that when criminal history is the first piece of information an employer sees, it dominates the evaluation and functions as a racial proxy. Delaying the inquiry forces employers to evaluate qualifications first, giving applicants a fair chance to demonstrate their fitness for the job before the stigma of a record intervenes.

**3. Fair chance housing protections.** Michigan should enact fair chance housing legislation prohibiting blanket criminal-record exclusions in public housing and publicly subsidized private housing. Landlords would be permitted to conduct individualized assessments after a conditional offer of tenancy but could not impose categorical bans (e.g., "no felony convictions"). Miller (2021) documents how housing exclusion is one of the most immediate and devastating barriers facing people leaving prison — without stable housing, maintaining employment, family connections, and compliance with parole conditions becomes nearly impossible. The U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development issued guidance in 2016 warning that blanket criminal-record bans may violate the Fair Housing Act's disparate-impact provisions, given the racial composition of the formerly incarcerated population, but this guidance has not been consistently enforced. State legislation would provide a clear, enforceable standard.

**4. Automatic restoration of voting rights upon release from incarceration.** Michigan should join the growing number of states that automatically restore voting rights when a person is released from prison, eliminating the current requirement that individuals complete parole and probation before regaining the franchise. Lee, Rowan, and Porter (2014) document how felony disenfranchisement strips civic participation from entire communities, particularly Black communities in which contact with the criminal justice system is concentrated. Disenfranchisement serves no penological purpose — it does not deter crime, incapacitate dangerous individuals, or rehabilitate offenders. What it does is reinforce the message that people with criminal records are permanent outsiders, unworthy of the most basic right of citizenship. Restoring the vote upon release signals that a person has paid their debt and is welcomed back into the political community.

---

## V. Conclusion

The American criminal justice system formally punishes people through sentences of incarceration, probation, and fines. But the most enduring punishment is informal and invisible: the criminal record that follows a person for life, barring them from jobs, housing, public benefits, and the ballot box. Sociological research — from Pager's audit studies to Western's and Miller's ethnographies to Kirk and Wakefield's cataloguing of collateral consequences — demonstrates that this system of permanent punishment is not race-neutral in its effects. It falls overwhelmingly on Black Americans, compounding the racial inequality that mass incarceration was built upon.

Michigan has already taken important steps. The state's recent expansion of expungement eligibility recognizes that people can change and that permanent exclusion serves no one — not the formerly incarcerated person, not their family, not their community, and not the state's economy. But petition-based expungement reaches too few people, and record-clearing alone is insufficient without complementary protections in employment, housing, and civic life.

The four reforms proposed here — automatic record sealing, ban-the-box employment protections, fair chance housing, and voting rights restoration — form a coherent package. Together, they dismantle the infrastructure of permanent punishment and replace it with a system that holds people accountable for their actions but does not condemn them forever. The sociological evidence is clear: most people desist from crime, criminal records are poor predictors of future behavior after a period of time, and the collateral consequences of conviction deepen poverty, destabilize families, and perpetuate racial inequality. Michigan has the opportunity to lead the nation in recognizing that a sentence, once served, should end.
