# Ending Mass Probation: A White Paper on Technical Revocations, Supervision Reform, and the Back Door to Prison in Georgia

**Prepared for:** The Georgia General Assembly

**Subject:** Reforming community supervision to cap probation terms, eliminate supervision fees, and replace incarceration for technical violations with graduated, supportive sanctions

---

## Executive Summary

Probation is widely understood as the lenient alternative to prison — a second chance, a path back to the community. In Georgia, that understanding is dangerously wrong. At its peak, the state supervised over 400,000 people on probation, giving it one of the largest probation populations in the nation. Sociological research reveals that this system does not function as mercy; it functions as a trap. Probation conditions — drug testing, curfews, mandatory treatment, employment requirements, and monthly supervision fees — are so numerous and so onerous that failure is nearly inevitable, particularly for the poor and for communities of color who are disproportionately supervised. When people fail, they are sent to prison not for committing new crimes but for "technical violations" of probation conditions: a missed appointment, a positive marijuana test, an unpaid fee. This paper identifies mass probation and technical revocations as a specific, measurable driver of Georgia's prison population. Drawing on sociological research and reform models already implemented in other states, it proposes three reforms: capping probation terms, eliminating supervision fees, and replacing incarceration for technical violations with graduated, community-based sanctions that reorient supervision from surveillance toward support.

## I. The Problem: Probation as Mass Punishment

The United States does not simply have a mass incarceration problem. It has a mass supervision problem. On any given day, approximately 3.5 million Americans are on probation — a number that dwarfs the 1.2 million in state and federal prison (Prison Policy Initiative, 2022). When parole is included, the total community supervision population exceeds 4.5 million. Yet probation receives a fraction of the public attention, political scrutiny, and scholarly analysis directed at prisons. Michelle Phelps (2016) calls this phenomenon "mass probation" and argues that it has developed its own logic, its own scale, and its own consequences — consequences that are largely invisible because probation is presumed to be benign.

Georgia exemplifies the problem. The state has historically maintained one of the largest probation populations in the country, supervising over 400,000 people at its peak — more than one in every twenty-five adults in some communities. Georgia's probation system is also heavily privatized: private probation companies supervise a substantial share of misdemeanor probationers, generating revenue through fees charged directly to the people they supervise (Human Rights Watch, 2016). The result is a system in which the entity responsible for supervision has a financial interest in extending it.

The conditions of probation are extensive. A typical probationer in Georgia must report to a probation officer regularly, submit to random drug testing, maintain employment, observe a curfew, attend mandatory treatment or classes, avoid contact with other people who have criminal records, and pay monthly supervision fees ranging from $50 to $100 — in addition to any court-ordered fines, restitution, and court costs (Human Rights Watch, 2016). Each of these conditions is individually plausible. Taken together, they constitute a web of obligations that is extraordinarily difficult to satisfy, particularly for people who are poor, who lack reliable transportation, who work irregular hours, or who struggle with addiction or mental illness — which is to say, the overwhelming majority of people on probation.

When a probationer fails to comply with any of these conditions, the result is a "technical violation." Technical violations are not new crimes. They are failures of bureaucratic compliance: a missed appointment, a failed drug test, an unpaid fee. Yet in Georgia, as in most states, a technical violation can result in revocation — the termination of probation and imposition of a prison sentence. Phelps (2018) argues that technical revocations have become a "back door to incarceration," responsible for a significant and growing share of prison admissions nationally. People who were never sentenced to prison — who were, in the eyes of the original sentencing judge, appropriate for community supervision — end up imprisoned anyway, not because they committed a new offense but because they could not navigate a system designed for failure.

## II. The Evidence: Who Fails, Why, and at What Cost

The burdens of mass probation do not fall equally. Alexander (2012) and Western and Pettit (2010) have documented how every stage of the American criminal justice system — from policing to sentencing to supervision — operates with profound racial disparities. Black Americans are supervised on probation at rates dramatically higher than white Americans, even after controlling for offense type. The National Academies report (Travis, Western, & Redburn, 2014) identifies community supervision as a key mechanism through which mass incarceration reproduces racial inequality: it is not merely that Black people are more likely to be placed on probation, but that the conditions of probation are harder to satisfy when you are poor, when you live in a neighborhood with limited public transportation, and when your employment options are already constrained by racial discrimination in hiring.

Supervision fees are a particularly insidious mechanism of failure. Harris, Evans, Beckett, and Wallace (2010) documented how legal financial obligations — fines, fees, and court costs — create cascading traps for low-income defendants. In the probation context, the dynamic is straightforward: a person who cannot afford $75 per month in supervision fees falls behind, the unpaid balance is treated as a violation, and the violation can trigger revocation. The fee does not protect public safety. It does not rehabilitate. It generates revenue for the supervising entity — often a private company — at the direct expense of the person being supervised. Clair (2020) further demonstrates that class position shapes every interaction with the criminal justice system: defendants with resources can hire attorneys to contest violations, negotiate modified conditions, or secure early termination, while poor defendants face the system alone and absorb its full punitive weight.

The consequences of revocation extend far beyond the individual. Kirk and Wakefield (2017) catalogue the collateral consequences of incarceration — job loss, housing instability, family separation, debt accumulation, and civic exclusion — and note that these consequences compound with repeated exposure. A person who is revoked to prison for a technical violation loses whatever employment, housing, and family stability they had managed to rebuild during probation, and when eventually released, begins the process again with deeper poverty and weaker social ties.

The fiscal cost to Georgia is substantial. Incarcerating a person costs the state tens of thousands of dollars per year. Every technical revocation that results in a prison sentence represents a direct expenditure that could be avoided if the violation were addressed through less costly, community-based responses. Georgia's own 2017 criminal justice reform legislation acknowledged this, implementing modest changes aimed at reducing revocations. But those reforms left the fundamental structure — open-ended probation terms, supervision fees, and the legal authority to imprison for technical violations — intact.

## III. Key Controversies

**Accountability and public safety.** The strongest objection to limiting revocations for technical violations is that probation conditions exist for a reason: they are meant to ensure that people under supervision remain law-abiding and engaged in rehabilitation. If violations go unaddressed, critics argue, probation becomes meaningless and public safety suffers. This concern is not frivolous, but it conflates two different things: responding to violations and responding with incarceration. Research on "swift and certain" sanctions demonstrates that consistent, moderate consequences — an evening in jail, increased reporting, mandatory community service — are more effective at reducing violations than the threat of a long prison term that is inconsistently applied (Hawken & Kleiman, 2009). The question is not whether violations should have consequences; it is whether prison is the appropriate consequence for missing an appointment or failing a drug test.

**"Probation is the lenient option."** Phelps (2016, 2018) challenges the deeply held assumption that probation is inherently lenient. For many probationers — particularly those who are poor, who have unstable housing, or who struggle with addiction — the conditions of probation are more burdensome than a short jail sentence would be. A sixty-day jail sentence ends; a five-year probation term is five years of surveillance, fees, and the constant threat of imprisonment. This is not a hypothetical concern: research shows that some defendants prefer short jail terms over probation precisely because probation's conditions are so onerous and its duration so unpredictable (Petersilia, 1990). When probation is experienced as more punitive than the alternative it supposedly replaces, something has gone fundamentally wrong.

**Net widening.** A subtler concern is that probation reform could paradoxically increase prison sentences if judges, deprived of the option to revoke for technical violations, simply sentence more people to prison in the first place. This risk is real but manageable. Reforms should be coupled with sentencing guidelines that preserve probation as the presumptive sentence for non-violent offenses and with data reporting requirements that allow legislators to monitor for net-widening effects.

**The abolitionist critique.** Scholars like Kaba (2021) and Davis (2003) argue that probation is itself a form of carceral control and that reforming its conditions merely makes the cage more comfortable. This paper's position is pragmatic: the 3.5 million people currently on probation cannot wait for systemic abolition. As Gottschalk (2015) argues, the scale of the carceral state demands action on multiple fronts simultaneously — reforms that demonstrably reduce incarceration are worth pursuing now.

## IV. Proposed Policy Reforms

This paper recommends that the Georgia General Assembly enact a three-part probation reform package:

**1. Cap probation terms.** Georgia should cap probation at 24 months for non-violent felonies and 12 months for misdemeanors, with automatic early termination after 12 months of compliance for felonies and 6 months for misdemeanors. Open-ended, multi-year probation terms — five, ten, or even twenty years for some offenses — serve no empirical purpose: research shows that the risk of reoffending declines most steeply in the first one to two years after sentencing, and that extended supervision beyond that period adds cost without reducing recidivism (Phelps, 2018). Shorter terms also reduce the cumulative probability of a technical violation — the longer a person is supervised, the more opportunities there are for something to go wrong. This approach has proven feasible in other states. Louisiana's 2017 Justice Reinvestment Act capped most felony probation at three years and created earned compliance credits allowing early termination, contributing to a significant reduction in the state's supervised population without compromising public safety (Pew Charitable Trusts, 2018). Mississippi's 2014 criminal justice reform similarly capped probation for drug and property offenses and introduced earned-discharge credits that incentivize compliance rather than merely punishing failure.

**2. Eliminate supervision fees.** Georgia should abolish all fees charged to probationers for the cost of their own supervision. Supervision fees do not promote rehabilitation or public safety; they generate revenue for supervising entities — often private companies — at the expense of the poorest defendants. Harris et al. (2010) demonstrate that financial obligations increase, rather than decrease, the likelihood of violation and revocation. The cost of supervision should be borne by the state, not imposed on the individuals being supervised. California's 2020 AB 1869 repealed 23 categories of criminal administrative fees — including supervision fees — recognizing them as both counterproductive and regressive (California Legislative Information, 2020). If Georgia cannot afford to supervise its current probation population without charging fees, that is an argument for reducing the size of the population, not for extracting money from people who do not have it.

**3. Replace incarceration for technical violations with graduated, supportive sanctions.** Georgia should prohibit imprisonment solely for technical violations of probation and simultaneously restructure the system's response from surveillance toward support. Rather than revoking probation and imposing a prison sentence when a person misses an appointment or fails a drug test, the state should require graduated, non-carceral sanctions: increased reporting frequency, community service, mandatory treatment referrals, and — only as a last resort — short-term jail stays of no more than 48 hours. Revocation to prison should be reserved for new criminal conduct. Critically, this shift requires reorienting the role of probation officers from compliance monitors to case managers who connect probationers with housing, employment assistance, and behavioral health treatment — interventions that address the underlying causes of criminal behavior rather than merely policing its symptoms (Travis et al., 2014). Texas offers a compelling precedent: in 2007, rather than building new prisons to accommodate projected growth, the legislature invested $241 million in treatment-based supervision and community alternatives to revocation, a strategy that reduced both the prison population and recidivism rates while saving the state billions in avoided construction and incarceration costs (Council of State Governments Justice Center, 2009). This reform eliminates the "back door to prison" that Phelps identifies while preserving meaningful accountability.

---

## V. Conclusion

Georgia has more people on probation than most countries have in their entire criminal justice systems. That fact alone demands scrutiny. When sociological research reveals that this vast supervisory apparatus sends tens of thousands of people to prison each year — not for committing new crimes but for missing appointments, failing drug tests, and being unable to pay fees — scrutiny becomes urgency.

Probation was designed to be a path back to the community. In its current form, it is a tripwire back to prison. The reforms proposed here — capping terms, eliminating fees, and replacing incarceration for technical violations with graduated, supportive sanctions — do not abolish probation. They restore it to its original purpose: a genuine alternative to incarceration, not an extension of it. And they are not speculative: Louisiana, Mississippi, California, and Texas have each enacted versions of these reforms, demonstrating that they are both politically feasible and empirically sound.

Georgia took a first step in 2017. The evidence demands that it finish the job.
