# SOC 2060 — White Paper Ideas: Criminal Justice Reform

> **Assignment prompt:** An elected official or governing body is interested in proposing criminal justice reform. Write a 5–7-page, double-spaced white paper for their state. Identify a specific problem and key controversies, then propose a solution or policy reform. Draw on sociological research, data, and course material.

## Idea 1 — Abolishing Legal Financial Obligations (LFOs): Ending the Criminalization of Poverty

**Elected official / governing body:** The Missouri State Legislature and Governor's Office — an especially apt audience given that the 2015 DOJ *Ferguson Police Department Report* exposed how that St. Louis suburb built an entire municipal budget on fines and fees extracted almost exclusively from Black residents.

**The problem:** Legal financial obligations — court costs, supervision fees, public-defender fees, restitution, and fines — have quietly become one of the most punishing features of the American criminal justice system. As Harris, Evans, Beckett, and Wallace (2010) show in "Drawing Blood from Stones," LFOs are overwhelmingly levied on defendants who are already poor, making full repayment functionally impossible. Unpaid balances compound through interest and surcharges, driver's licenses get suspended, warrants are issued, and people are jailed — not for the original offense, but for being too poor to pay. The Ferguson report documented a system in which police and courts operated as a revenue-generating apparatus targeting Black residents for minor infractions, generating predictable resentment and eroding all civic trust in law enforcement. Matthew Clair's *Privilege and Punishment* (2020) adds the class dimension: defendants with access to private attorneys can negotiate outcomes that poor defendants, relying on overwhelmed public defenders, simply cannot. Together, these works reveal that LFOs are not neutral cost-recovery tools — they are a mechanism that converts misdemeanor poverty into a permanent entanglement with the carceral state.

**Proposed solutions:** (1) Mandatory ability-to-pay hearings before any LFO is imposed, with a presumption of waiver for defendants below 200% of the federal poverty line. (2) Legislation eliminating *user fees* (fees charged to fund the court system itself) — courts should be publicly funded, not self-financing through defendants. (3) Substituting community service for fines when defendants cannot pay. (4) An amnesty program clearing existing LFO debt that has been delinquent for five or more years, with automatic reinstatement of suspended licenses. This would reduce jail populations, save enforcement costs, and — as the Ferguson evidence demands — begin to rebuild legitimacy in communities that have been systematically exploited.

## Idea 2 — Reforming Mass Probation: From Surveillance to Support

**Elected official / governing body:** The Georgia or Texas State Legislature — both states have among the largest probation populations in the country and have already begun bipartisan reform conversations, making them realistic audiences for a reform proposal.

**The problem:** Probation is often treated as a lenient alternative to incarceration, but Michelle Phelps's research — "Mass Probation" (2016) and "Ending Mass Probation" (2018) — reveals it is itself a form of mass punishment. More than 4.5 million Americans are on probation or parole, dwarfing the prison population. Conditions can be extraordinarily onerous: drug testing, curfews, GPS monitoring, mandatory treatment participation, regular check-ins, and supervision fees that can run hundreds of dollars a month. Human Rights Watch (2016) documented how these conditions set people up to fail: a missed appointment due to a work shift, a positive drug test, or an unpaid fee can trigger revocation and a prison sentence — not for a new crime, but for a *technical violation*. Bruce Western's and Devah Pager's work further shows that the people most likely to be on probation — poor Black and Latino men — are also those least likely to have stable employment, transportation, or housing that makes compliance easy. Probation, in short, has become an extended arm of incarceration rather than a genuine pathway back to the community.

**Proposed solutions:** (1) Cap probation terms at 24 months for all non-violent felonies and 12 months for misdemeanors. (2) Eliminate supervision fees, which evidence shows increase rather than decrease the likelihood of violation. (3) Prohibit incarceration for technical violations (as opposed to new criminal conduct) after the first revocation — sanctions short of prison (brief jail stays, increased reporting) can provide accountability without the collateral devastation of a prison sentence. (4) Shift supervision resources toward connecting probationers with housing, employment, and treatment, rather than monitoring for violations. Research on "swift and certain" sanctions suggests that consistent, moderate responses reduce recidivism more than the threat of lengthy imprisonment.

## Idea 3 — Prosecutorial Accountability and the Hidden Driver of Mass Incarceration

**Elected official / governing body:** A State Legislature's Judiciary Committee, or a newly elected reform-minded District Attorney — this topic is equally relevant to legislators who can mandate data collection and to DAs who can implement charging guidelines internally.

**The problem:** The standard story of what caused mass incarceration — the war on drugs, mandatory minimums, tough-on-crime politics — is importantly incomplete. John Pfaff's *Locked In* (2017) makes the provocative but well-supported argument that prosecutors are the central, underappreciated driver. Between 1994 and 2008, as crime fell and arrests declined, the probability that a prosecutor would file felony charges against an arrested person *rose dramatically*. This prosecutorial inflation of case severity went largely unnoticed because prosecutors are among the least-scrutinized actors in the system. Mona Lynch's *Hard Bargains* (2016) showed that prosecutorial culture and local politics vary enormously, producing wildly different outcomes for defendants with identical facts in different counties. William Stuntz (2004) analyzed how the collapse of plea bargaining's shadow — the real possibility of trial — gives prosecutors near-total power. And HRW's (2016) report on coerced guilty pleas documented how the sentencing discount for pleading guilty versus going to trial is so extreme that innocent defendants routinely plead guilty rather than risk a trial. The result is a system in which enormous power is concentrated in an office that is almost entirely unaccountable — DAs face election every four years, almost never lose, and disclose virtually no data about their charging or plea decisions.

**Proposed solutions:** (1) Mandate that prosecutors' offices publicly report charging, plea, and sentencing data disaggregated by race, offense type, and case outcome — creating the accountability that currently does not exist. (2) Establish an independent prosecutorial oversight body (as in some European systems) empowered to investigate patterns of racial disparity. (3) Require "racial impact statements" before new mandatory-minimum legislation, similar to environmental impact reviews. (4) Reform mandatory minimums that strip judges of discretion and hand it to prosecutors — moving sentencing power back toward the judiciary reduces the coercive leverage behind plea offers. These measures do not require abolition of prosecutorial discretion; they require that it be exercised transparently and accountably.

## Idea 4 — Ending Cash Bail and Pretrial Detention Reform

**Elected official / governing body:** A State Legislature's Criminal Justice Reform Committee, or a State Supreme Court Chief Justice — because bail reform requires both legislative action (changing statutes) and judicial buy-in (changing court practice).

**The problem:** The cash bail system detains presumptively innocent people based on one thing: whether they can afford to pay. The Marshall Project's explainer on bail makes clear that the United States is one of very few countries in the world that relies on a commercial bail bond industry to determine who awaits trial in a cage and who goes home. A wealthy defendant charged with a violent offense is released; a poor defendant charged with shoplifting sits in jail for weeks or months awaiting trial. Clair's *Privilege and Punishment* (2020) documents how this pretrial detention then shapes every subsequent outcome: detained defendants are more likely to plead guilty (sometimes to charges they did not commit, just to get out), receive longer sentences, lose jobs, and lose housing. Stuntz (2004) connects this to the plea bargain system: detention is itself a coercive tool that makes defendants accept worse plea offers. The National Academies Report (Travis et al., 2014) documents the downstream social costs — family separation, employment loss, housing instability — that flow from even brief pretrial detention. The "whole pie" of mass incarceration (Prison Policy Initiative, 2022) makes visible how many people are actually held pretrial, a number that rivals the sentenced population and is largely invisible in policy debates.

**Proposed solutions:** (1) Establish a presumption of pretrial release for all non-violent offenses, with the burden on the state to demonstrate that detention is necessary. (2) Replace cash bail with evidence-based risk assessment — not to expand detention but to ensure detention decisions are based on genuine danger or flight risk, not wealth. (3) Invest in pretrial services offices that provide reminders, transportation, and case-management support to help defendants make court appearances without incarceration. (4) Require expedited trials for detained defendants — the right to a speedy trial should be enforceable, not merely nominal. New Jersey's 2017 bail reform, which largely eliminated cash bail, provides a model: pretrial detention rates fell without increasing crime.

## Idea 5 — Decriminalizing Drug Possession: From the War on Drugs to a Public Health Model

**Elected official / governing body:** A State Legislature or a Governor's Drug Policy Advisory Committee — particularly in a state that has not yet moved toward decriminalization and still prosecutes possession heavily, such as Texas, Louisiana (see Chang, 2012), or Florida.

**The problem:** The war on drugs has been one of the most consequential — and least effective — drivers of mass incarceration in American history. Craig Reinarman (1997) showed how "drug scares" are socially constructed: the crack cocaine panic of the 1980s was fueled by racialized media coverage and political opportunism, not by epidemiological necessity, and it produced sentencing disparities (crack vs. powder cocaine: 100:1) that were explicitly racial in their application. Michelle Alexander's *The New Jim Crow* (2012) extends this argument: the war on drugs functioned as a mechanism of racial control, sweeping Black men out of communities and into a caste system of permanent legal marginalization. Johann Hari's work (2015) draws on the Portuguese experiment, which in 2001 decriminalized personal possession of all drugs and redirected savings toward treatment — resulting in falling HIV transmission, falling overdose death, and falling drug use. The Justice Policy Institute (2011) critiqued drug courts as insufficient half-measures that widen the net of criminal supervision without addressing the structural causes of drug use. And Angela Davis (2003) frames the entire edifice as a product of the prison-industrial complex, which requires a steady supply of bodies to remain profitable. Meanwhile, Devah Pager's work on criminal records shows that a single drug conviction can cost a person employment opportunities for decades.

**Proposed solutions:** (1) Decriminalize personal-use possession of all controlled substances — removing criminal penalties while preserving civil citations and mandatory health referrals. (2) Reinvest the savings from reduced prosecution and incarceration into community-based treatment, harm reduction (needle exchange, naloxone distribution), and housing-first programs. (3) Enact automatic expungement of existing possession convictions, removing the collateral consequences that follow people long after their sentence ends. (4) Mandate racial impact review of any future drug enforcement policy. Oregon's Measure 110 (2020) provides a recent case study — and its implementation challenges offer lessons about pairing decriminalization with adequate treatment infrastructure.

## Idea 6 — Reentry Reform: Dismantling Permanent Punishment Through Record Sealing and Fair Chance Policies

**Elected official / governing body:** A Governor's Office and State Legislature working in tandem — because comprehensive reentry reform requires executive action (pardon/expungement policies), legislative action (sealing statutes, fair-chance housing laws), and administrative action (licensing boards, public housing authorities).

**The problem:** The formal sentence — the years served in prison — is only the beginning of punishment in the American system. Devah Pager's landmark 2003 audit study, "The Mark of a Criminal Record," sent matched pairs of applicants with and without criminal records (and of different races) to apply for real jobs. The results were stunning: a criminal record reduced callback rates by 50% for white applicants — and Black applicants *with no record* fared worse than white applicants *with* a record, revealing the compounding of race and criminalization. Bruce Western's *Homeward* (2018) and Reuben Miller's *Halfway Home* (2021) document the texture of reentry in rich ethnographic detail: the impossibility of finding housing, the exclusion from professional licenses, the surveillance and stigma that follow people out of prison. Kirk and Wakefield (2017) catalogued the breadth of *collateral consequences* — legal barriers to employment, housing, education, public benefits, and voting that attach automatically to a conviction, often without any judge ever pronouncing them. Lee, Rowan, and Porter (2014) documented how felony disenfranchisement strips civic participation from large portions of Black communities in some states. Together these works describe a system of permanent, invisible punishment that operates entirely outside the formal sentencing structure — and that sociologists have only recently begun to measure.

**Proposed solutions:** (1) Automatic record *sealing* (not just expungement on petition) after a defined crime-free period — research shows most people desist from crime within 7 years of their last conviction, and automatic processes reach people who lack the resources to navigate a petition process. (2) "Ban the box" legislation for both public *and* private employers, delaying criminal history inquiries until a conditional job offer has been made — giving applicants the chance to be evaluated on their qualifications first. (3) Fair chance housing protections prohibiting blanket exclusions in public and subsidized private housing based on criminal history. (4) Automatic restoration of voting rights upon release from incarceration — eliminating post-supervision disenfranchisement periods that serve no penological purpose. These reforms address what Western and Pettit (2010) call the "invisible inequality" produced by mass incarceration: consequences that statisticians and policymakers routinely fail to count but that are among the most durable.
