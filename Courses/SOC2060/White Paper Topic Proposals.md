## Proposal 1 — Abolishing Legal Financial Obligations: Ending the Criminalization of Poverty in Missouri

This white paper is addressed to the **Missouri State Legislature**, urging comprehensive reform of legal financial obligations (LFOs) — the fines, fees, court costs, and surcharges imposed on criminal defendants. **The specific problem is that courts across Missouri have transformed the criminal justice system into a revenue-generating apparatus that systematically punishes people for being poor.** Low-income defendants — disproportionately Black — are assessed fines and fees they cannot pay; those unpaid balances then compound through interest and surcharges, trigger driver's license suspensions, generate arrest warrants, and ultimately lead to re-incarceration — not for the original offense but for the inability to pay. The result is a debt trap that converts minor infractions into permanent entanglement with the carceral state. The 2015 Department of Justice investigation of the Ferguson Police Department exposed this mechanism in stark detail: police stops, citations, and court appearances functioned primarily as revenue extraction, falling overwhelmingly on Black residents (DOJ, 2015). Harris, Evans, Beckett, and Wallace (2010) documented how LFOs are functionally uncollectable from poor defendants yet generate cascading legal penalties — what they call "drawing blood from stones." Clair (2020) further demonstrates that class position profoundly shapes a defendant's experience in court, with wealthier defendants able to negotiate outcomes that are structurally unavailable to the poor. This paper proposes a four-part reform: (1) mandatory ability-to-pay hearings before any LFO is imposed, with a presumption of waiver for defendants below 200% of the federal poverty line; (2) elimination of court user fees so that the justice system is publicly funded rather than self-financing through the people it processes; (3) substitution of community service for fines when defendants demonstrate financial hardship; and (4) an amnesty program clearing LFO debt that has been delinquent for five or more years, paired with automatic reinstatement of suspended licenses. Together, these measures would dismantle a system that punishes people for being poor and Black rather than for posing any genuine public safety threat.

## Proposal 2 — Reforming Mass Probation: Ending Technical Revocations in Georgia

This white paper is addressed to the **Georgia General Assembly**, proposing reform of the state's probation system — one of the largest in the nation, supervising over 400,000 people at its peak. **The specific problem is that probation, widely perceived as a lenient alternative to prison, has become a mass-punishment system in its own right — one that sends tens of thousands of people to prison each year not for committing new crimes but for failing to comply with bureaucratic conditions they were set up to fail.** A missed appointment because of a work shift, a positive marijuana test, or an unpaid monthly supervision fee of $50–100 can trigger a "technical violation" and revocation to prison. This is what Phelps (2016) terms "mass probation": over 4.5 million Americans under community supervision, disproportionately poor people of color, trapped under conditions so onerous that failure is almost inevitable. Human Rights Watch (2016) documented how probation conditions — drug testing, curfews, mandatory treatment attendance, employment requirements, and fees — function not as support but as tripwires. Phelps (2018) argues that technical revocations have become a "back door" to incarceration, responsible for a significant share of prison admissions nationally. The collateral consequences documented by Western and Pettit (2010) — lost employment, family disruption, deepening poverty — compound with each revocation cycle, making each subsequent period of supervision even harder to complete. This paper proposes: (1) capping probation terms at 24 months for non-violent felonies and 12 months for misdemeanors, with automatic early termination for compliance; (2) eliminating supervision fees, which research shows increase violation rates rather than promoting accountability; (3) prohibiting incarceration solely for technical violations, requiring instead that graduated, non-carceral sanctions (increased reporting, brief community service, mandatory treatment referrals) be exhausted first; and (4) reorienting probation officers' role from surveillance toward connecting probationers with housing, employment, and behavioral health services. Georgia has already taken initial steps in this direction through its 2017 criminal justice reform package; this paper argues for completing that work by addressing the structural incentives that continue to drive revocations.

## Proposal 3 — Prosecutorial Accountability: Transparent Charging and Racial Impact Review

This white paper is addressed to a **State Legislature's Judiciary Committee** (applicable to any state, but particularly relevant in states with wide county-to-county disparities in incarceration rates, such as Florida, California, or Pennsylvania). **The specific problem is that local prosecutors — the single most powerful actors in the American criminal justice system — exercise virtually unchecked discretion over who gets charged, with what offense, and on what terms, and this unaccountable power is the primary but least-recognized driver of mass incarceration and its racial disparities.** Unlike judges, whose sentences are governed by guidelines, and police, whose conduct is subject to Fourth Amendment review, prosecutors make charging and plea decisions behind closed doors with no transparency, no data reporting, and almost no oversight. Pfaff (2017) demonstrates that between 1994 and 2008, even as crime rates fell and arrests declined, the rate at which prosecutors filed felony charges against arrested individuals *rose sharply* — inflating the severity of the system's response with no external check. Lynch (2016) shows that this discretion is wildly inconsistent: prosecutorial culture varies dramatically by county, meaning a defendant's fate depends as much on geography as on the facts of the case. The plea bargaining system compounds this power imbalance. Stuntz (2004) documents how the collapse of meaningful trial prospects — fewer than 5% of federal cases go to trial — gives prosecutors near-total leverage over defendants, while HRW (2016) shows that the resulting plea deals are frequently coerced, with defendants pleading guilty to charges they would contest at trial simply because the "trial penalty" (the difference between the plea offer and the post-trial sentence) is catastrophically large. Forman (2012) adds that racial disparities are deeply embedded in charging decisions, even when controlling for offense severity. This paper proposes: (1) mandating that all prosecutors' offices publish annual data on charging decisions, plea offers, and sentencing outcomes, disaggregated by race, offense type, and county; (2) establishing an independent prosecutorial oversight commission empowered to investigate patterns of disparity and issue binding recommendations; (3) requiring racial impact statements for all new criminal penalty legislation, modeled on environmental impact reviews; and (4) reforming mandatory minimum statutes to restore meaningful judicial discretion at sentencing, thereby reducing the coercive leverage that prosecutors currently wield through charge selection. These reforms do not eliminate prosecutorial discretion — they make it visible and accountable for the first time.

## Proposal 4 — Eliminating Cash Bail: Pretrial Justice Reform

This white paper is addressed to the **Illinois General Assembly and the Illinois Supreme Court** (Illinois enacted the Pretrial Fairness Act in 2023, making it a timely audience for a paper evaluating and strengthening that reform, though the proposal could be adapted to any state that has not yet acted). **The specific problem is that the cash bail system detains hundreds of thousands of legally innocent people before trial based on a single factor — whether they can afford to pay — and this wealth-based detention destroys lives, coerces guilty pleas from innocent defendants, and deepens racial and economic inequality without improving public safety.** A wealthy defendant charged with assault walks free; a poor defendant charged with shoplifting sits in jail for weeks or months awaiting trial. The Marshall Project's reporting on bail lays out this two-tiered system plainly. But the downstream consequences are what make pretrial detention so destructive: detained defendants lose their jobs, lose their housing, and — facing the choice between months in jail awaiting trial or immediate release on a guilty plea — routinely plead guilty to charges they would contest if they were free, even when they are innocent (Stuntz, 2004; HRW, 2016). Clair (2020) documents how class permeates every stage of court processing, and the National Academies report (Travis et al., 2014) details the cascading consequences of even brief incarceration on employment, housing, health, and family stability. The Prison Policy Initiative's "Whole Pie" analysis (2022) reveals that a substantial fraction of the incarcerated population on any given day consists of people who have not been convicted of anything — they are legally presumed innocent but functionally punished. This paper proposes: (1) establishing a statutory presumption of pretrial release for all non-violent offenses, with the burden on the prosecution to demonstrate by clear and convincing evidence that detention is necessary to prevent flight or protect public safety; (2) replacing cash bail with validated, transparent risk assessment instruments — used not to expand detention but to ensure that detention decisions are evidence-based rather than wealth-based; (3) investing in pretrial services programs that provide court-date reminders, transportation assistance, and case management to help defendants meet their obligations without incarceration; and (4) enforcing the constitutional right to a speedy trial by requiring expedited proceedings for any defendant who remains detained pretrial. New Jersey's 2017 bail reform, which largely eliminated cash bail and saw pretrial jail populations drop by 44% with no increase in crime, provides the primary policy model.

## Proposal 5 — Decriminalizing Drug Possession: A Public Health Alternative to the War on Drugs

This white paper is addressed to the **Louisiana State Legislature**, a state where Chang (2012) documented an incarceration rate that is the highest in the nation and where drug offenses remain a leading driver of prison admissions. **The specific problem is that criminalizing drug possession — arresting, prosecuting, and imprisoning people for using or carrying controlled substances — has driven mass incarceration for five decades without reducing drug use, improving public health, or making communities safer, while devastating Black and brown communities through racially selective enforcement.** Louisiana imprisons people for simple possession at rates that dwarf the rest of the developed world, yet the state's addiction rates, overdose deaths, and drug-related harms remain among the worst in the nation. The policy has failed on its own terms. Reinarman (1997) demonstrates that American drug scares are socially constructed: the crack cocaine panic of the 1980s was driven by racialized media framing and political opportunism, producing the 100:1 crack-to-powder sentencing disparity that Alexander (2012) identifies as central to the "New Jim Crow" — a system of racial control operating through criminal law rather than explicit racial statute. Hari (2015) draws on the experience of Portugal, which in 2001 decriminalized personal possession of all drugs and invested the savings in treatment and harm reduction; the result was declining overdose deaths, declining HIV transmission, and stable or declining drug use — demonstrating that a public health approach achieves what criminalization cannot. The Justice Policy Institute (2011) argues that drug courts, while well-intentioned, are an insufficient response because they widen the net of criminal supervision rather than addressing the underlying public health crisis, and Davis (2003) frames the entire apparatus as a function of the prison-industrial complex's need for a steady supply of incarcerated people. This paper proposes: (1) decriminalizing personal-use possession of all controlled substances — replacing criminal penalties with civil citations and mandatory referral to health assessment; (2) redirecting savings from reduced prosecution and incarceration into community-based treatment, harm reduction services (syringe exchange programs, naloxone distribution), and housing-first programs; (3) enacting automatic expungement of prior simple possession convictions, removing the collateral consequences that Pager (2003) shows follow people for decades after their sentence ends; and (4) requiring racial impact review of any future drug enforcement policy to prevent the discriminatory enforcement patterns that Alexander documents. Louisiana's extreme incarceration rate and heavy reliance on drug prosecutions make it both a high-need and a high-impact setting for this reform.

## Proposal 6 — Reentry Reform: Dismantling Permanent Punishment Through Record Sealing and Fair Chance Policies

This white paper is addressed to the **Governor of Michigan and the Michigan State Legislature**, a state that has recently expanded expungement eligibility and is positioned to become a national model — but whose current reforms still leave significant gaps. **The specific problem is that a criminal record operates as a sentence that never ends: it permanently bars people from employment, housing, professional licenses, public benefits, and the right to vote — punishments that no judge ever imposed, that persist decades after the formal sentence is complete, and that fall disproportionately on Black Americans.** An estimated 70 to 100 million Americans carry some form of criminal record, and for them, "paying one's debt to society" is a fiction — the debt follows them for life. Pager's (2003) landmark audit study quantified this: a criminal record reduced job callbacks by 50% for white applicants and by nearly two-thirds for Black applicants. Most strikingly, Black applicants *without* criminal records received fewer callbacks than white applicants *with* felony convictions, revealing how racial stigma and criminal-record stigma compound into a double exclusion. Miller's *Halfway Home* (2021) and Western's *Homeward* (2018) provide ethnographic accounts of the reentry experience: the fruitless job searches, the housing applications rejected on background check, the exclusion from licensed professions (barbers, nurses, plumbers), the surveillance of parole, and the social death of permanent stigma. Kirk and Wakefield (2017) catalogued the sheer breadth of these collateral consequences — over 40,000 legal restrictions attached to criminal convictions across the fifty states — most imposed automatically, without judicial consideration. Lee, Rowan, and Porter (2014) document how felony disenfranchisement laws strip civic participation from entire communities, particularly in states that extend voting bans beyond incarceration to include parole and probation. This paper proposes: (1) automatic record sealing — not petition-based expungement, which requires legal knowledge and resources that most formerly incarcerated people lack — after a defined crime-free period (research suggests 7 years is appropriate for most offenses, as desistance rates plateau after that point); (2) "ban the box" legislation for both public and private employers, delaying criminal history disclosure until after a conditional offer is made so applicants can be evaluated on qualifications before being screened by their record; (3) fair chance housing protections that prohibit blanket criminal-record bans in public and subsidized private housing; and (4) automatic restoration of voting rights upon release from incarceration, eliminating the post-supervision disenfranchisement periods that serve no penological purpose. These reforms address what Western and Pettit (2010) call the "invisible inequality" produced by mass incarceration — consequences that disappear from statistics but shape millions of lives.
